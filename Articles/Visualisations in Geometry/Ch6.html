6.1 Lie Groups and Lie Algebras

In this section we will give some (by no means comprehensive) background on the basics of Lie theory. The definitions and results come from [HAL15] and [HAM17].

6.1.1 Lie Groups

Definition 6.1.1.

A Lie group is a smooth manifold G together with smooth maps

ρ:G×G →G ι:G →G
(g,h) ↦g⁢h g ↦g−1

and an element e such that for all g,h,k∈G,

(g⁢h)⁢k=g⁢(h⁢k)
g⁢e=g=e⁢g
g⁢g−1=e=g−1⁢g.

A readily available class of Lie groups are the matrix Lie groups. Let 𝕂 be a field ℝ or ℂ.

Definition 6.1.2.

Let G be a Lie group that is a closed subset of GL⁢(n,𝕂). Then we call G a matrix Lie group.

An important result for understanding compact Lie groups is

Theorem 6.1.1.

Let G be a compact Lie group. Then there exists a smooth, injective homomorphism ϕ of G into a general linear group GL⁢(n,ℝ) for some n∈ℕ.

This is saying that every compact Lie group has a faithful representation 6.1.11.

For any element g∈G, we obtain invertible diffeomorphisms Lg,Rg,Cg from G to itself.

Definition 6.1.3.

Let G be a Lie group, and g∈G. Then we define the left multiplication, right multiplication, and conjugation maps by

Lg:G →G Rg:G →G Cg:G →G
h ↦g⁢h h ↦h⁢g h ↦g⁢h⁢g−1

The left and right multiplication maps as well as the conjugation map are all diffeomorphisms, with inverses Lg−1,Rg−1,Cg−1 respectively. Furthermore, Cg is a group isomorphism for all g∈G. These maps for the Lie groups SO⁢(3) and SU⁢(2) are visualised in sections 3.2 and 4.2.

6.1.2 Lie Algebras

Closely related to the notion of Lie group, is that of a Lie Algebra.

Definition 6.1.4.

A Lie algebra is a vector space V along with a bilinear, skew-symmetric map [⋅,⋅]:V×V→V such that

[X,[Y,Z]]+[Z,[X,Y]]+[Y,[Z,X]]=0∀X,Y,Z∈V. (6.1)

Equation (6.1) is called the Jacobi identity.

Example 6.1.1.

The set of all vector fields Γ⁢(T⁢M) on a smooth manifold M forms an infinite-dimensional Lie Algebra under the bracket (X,Y)↦[X,Y] where [X,Y]⁢(f)=X⁢(Y⁢(f))−Y⁢(X⁢(f)).

So for any smooth manifold M we have an infinite-dimensional Lie algebra consisting of all vector fields on M. But given a Lie group G, we can obtain a finite dimensional Lie Algebra Lie⁡(G)=𝔤 with dimension equal to that of G. This construction is fundamental to the study of Lie groups.

Keeping with the convention that Lie groups are denoted with upper case letters GL⁢(n,ℝ), SL⁢(n,ℝ), SO⁢(n,ℝ), SU⁢(n), we will denote their corresponding Lie algebras in lower case fraktur 𝔤⁢𝔩, 𝔰⁢𝔩, 𝔰⁢𝔬, 𝔰⁢𝔲, etc.

In order to define Lie⁡(G)=𝔤, we use left-invariant vector fields.

Definition 6.1.5.

A left-invariant vector field X∈Γ⁢(T⁢M) is a vector field such that for all g∈G,

(Lg)∗⁢(X)⁢(f)=X⁢(f).

We will denote the set of left-invariant vector fields on a Lie group by L.

Given a Lie group G, left-invariant vector fields always exist, and are determined uniquely by their value at the identity of G, and because of this, we are able to construct a vector space isomorphism between the set of left-invariant vector fields on a Lie Group to it’s tangent space at the identity.

Proposition 6.1.1.

Let G be a Lie group. The set L of left invariant vector fields on G is a vector space isomorphic to Te⁢G.

Proof.

Let X,Y be left-invariant vector fields and λ be a scalar. Then for all g∈G we have

((Lg)∗⁢(X+λ⁢Y))⁢(f) =((Lg)∗⁢X)⁢(f)+(λ⁢(Lg)∗⁢Y)⁢(f)
=X⁢(f)+λ⁢Y⁢(f)

by definition of left-invariance. Therefore the left-invariant vector fields form a vector space. To obtain our isomorphism with Te⁢G, we have map

Φ:L →Te⁢G
X ↦Xe

and

Φ−1:Te⁢G →L
v ↦V

where Vg=(Lg)∗⁢v∈TLg⁢(e)⁢G=Tg⁢G. The map Φ is linear because addition and scalar multiplication of vector spaces is defined pointwise. We have that

Φ⁢(Φ−1⁢(v)) =Φ⁢(g↦(Lg)∗⁢v)
=(Le)∗⁢v
=id∗⁡v
=v

and

Φ−1⁢(Φ⁢(X)) =Φ−1⁢(Xe)=V

where Vg=(Lg)∗⁢Xe=Xg by definition of left-invariance. ∎

The above proof is included because it shows us how to obtain a left-invariant vector field from an element of Te⁢G=𝔤, and how to obtain an element of 𝔤 from a left-invariant vector field.

To define the Lie bracket on 𝔤, turning 𝔤 from just a vector space into a Lie algebra, we use the identification of 𝔤 with the set of left invariant vector fields on G, and then use the usual bracket of vector fields.

Definition 6.1.6.

Let x,y∈𝔤 and X,Y∈Γ⁢(T⁢M) be the unique left invariant vector fields with values Xe=x,Ye=y at the identity. Then

[⋅,⋅]:𝔤×𝔤 →𝔤
(x,y) ↦[X,Y]e

That is we take [x,y] to be the value at the identity of the vector field [X,Y].

6.1.3 Exponential Map

Suppose we have a Lie group G with Lie algebra 𝔤. The exponential map is a way of going from 𝔤 to G. It takes a vector 𝐯∈𝔤=Te⁢G and outputs the result of flowing along the left invariant vector field determined by 𝐯 for time 1. Let M be a smooth manifold and X a smooth vector field on M.

Definition 6.1.7.

A curve γ:I→M, where I⊆ℝ is an open interval around 0, is called an integral curve for X through p∈M if

γ⁢(0)=p⁢ and ⁢γ′⁢(t)=Xγ⁢(t)⁢∀t∈I.
Theorem 6.1.2.

For all p∈M, there exists an open neighbourhood U of p in M and an open interval I containing 0 such that the integral curves γq are defined on I for all q∈U. The map

ΦU:U×I →M
(q,t) ↦γq⁢(t)

is differentiable and is called the local flow of X.

Definition 6.1.8.

Let X∈𝔤 be a left-invariant vector field and let ΦX be the integral curve through through e∈G. Then we define the exponential map

exp:𝔤 →G (6.2)
X ↦exp⁡(X)=exp⁡X=ΦX⁢(1).

Left-invariant vector fields always have global flows (defined on all of ℝ), so this definition makes sense, i.e. there is no risk of ΦX being undefined at 1. The exponential map for a Lie group is closely related to the idea of a matrix exponential.

Definition 6.1.9.

Let A∈Mat⁡(n×n,𝕂). Then the exponential of A is defined as the sum

eA=∑k=0∞1k!⁢Ak. (6.3)

We may be worried that this sum does not converge, however just as in the case of the real exponential function, it does.

Lemma 6.1.1.

For any square matrix A∈Mat⁡(n×n,𝕂), the series ∑k=0∞1k!⁢Ak converges.

By the following theorem we see where the exponential map 6.2 gets it’s name. The exponential map of a matrix group is exactly given by the formula (6.3) for the matrix exponential.

Theorem 6.1.3.

Let A∈𝔤⁢𝔩⁢(n,𝕂)⊂Mat⁡(n×n,𝕂). Then

exp⁡(A)=eA (6.4)

where exp on the left denotes the canonical exponential map from Lie algebra to Lie group (6.2). The same formula holds for the exponential map of any linear group.

An inverse to the matrix exponential is given by

Definition 6.1.10.

Let A be an n×n matrix. Then the logarithm of A is

log⁡A=∑k=1∞(−1)k+1⁢(A−𝕀)kk (6.5)

wherever the series 6.5 converges.

For connected Lie groups, the exponential map is surjective. It is always a diffeomorphism in some neighbourhood of the identity. This gives a chart containing the identity in terms of logarithmic coordinates.

6.1.4 Representations

We can study Lie groups and Lie algebras by how they map into the general linear groups of vector spaces.

Definition 6.1.11.

Let G be a matrix Lie group. A finite-dimensional complex (real) representation of G is a Lie group homomorphism

Π:G→GL⁢(V)

where V is a finite-dimensional complex (real) vector space (with dim(V)≥1). If 𝔤 is real or complex Lie algebra, then a finite-dimensional complex (real) representation of 𝔤 is a Lie algebra homomorphism π:𝔤→𝔤⁢𝔩⁢(V), where V is a finite-dimensional complex (real) vector space.If Π or π is an injective homomorphism, the representation is called faithful.

There is a notion of maps between representations as follows:

Definition 6.1.12.

Let G be a matrix Lie group, let Π be a representation of G acting on the space V, and let Σ be a representation of G acting on the space W. A linear map ϕ:V→W is called an intertwining map of representations if

ϕ⁢(Π⁢(A)⁢v)=Σ⁢(A)⁢ϕ⁢(v)

for all A∈G and all v∈V. The analogous property defines intertwining maps of representations of a Lie algebra.

If ϕ is an intertwining map of representations, and ϕ is invertible, then ϕ is called an isomorphism of representations. If there exists an isomorphism between V and W, then the representations are said to be isomorphic.

Given a representation of a matrix Lie group, we can construct a representation of it’s Lie algebra.

Proposition 6.1.2.

Let G be a matrix Lie group with Lie algebra 𝔤 and let Π be a (finite-dimensional real or complex) representation of G, acting on the space V. Then there is a unique representation π of 𝔤 acting on the same space such that

Π⁢(eX)=eπ⁢(X)

for all X∈𝔤. The representation π can be computed as

π⁢(X)=dd⁢t⁢Π⁢(et⁢X)|t=0

and satisfies

π⁢(A⁢X⁢A−1)=Π⁢(A)⁢π⁢(X)⁢Π⁢(A)−1

for all X∈𝔤 and A∈G.

We then have a way of determining isomorphism of Lie group representations (for connected matrix Lie groups anyway) through their associated Lie algebra representations, which can be easier to work with than the group representations themselves, since the Lie algebras in question are well-behaved finite-dimensional vector spaces.

Proposition 6.1.3.

Let G be a connected matrix Lie group, let Π1 and Π2 be representations of G, and let π1,π2 be the associated Lie algebra representations. Then π1 and π2 are isomorphic if and only if Π1 and Π2 are isomorphic.

6.2 Quaternions

Somewhat backwards to typical fashion, where quaternions find applications in computer graphics [VIN21], we are applying computer graphics to visualising quaternions as they appear in representing SU⁢(2).

Following [GIR07], we introduce quaternions. To do so, we first define the quaternion group.

Definition 6.2.1.

The quaternion group Q is the 8 element set {1,−1,i,−i,j,−j,k,−k} equipped with multiplication such that the relations

i2 =j2=k2=−1,
i⁢j =k

hold.

With the quaternion group we are now ready to define the quaternions (or the quaternion algebra).

Definition 6.2.2.

The quaternions ℍ are the vector space {a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k|a0,a1,a2,a3∈ℝ}. For quaternions a=(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k),b=(b0+b1⁢i+b2⁢j+b3⁢k) and λ∈ℝ we define addition by

(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k)+(b0+b1⁢i+b2⁢j+b3⁢k)=(a0+b0)+(a1+b1)⁢i+(a2+b2)⁢j+(a3+b3)⁢k

and scalar multiplication by

λ⁢(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k)=(λ⁢a0)+(λ⁢a1)⁢i+(λ⁢a2)⁢j+(λ⁢a3)⁢k.

They form an associative algebra with multiplication operation

a⁢b= (a0⁢b0−a1⁢b1−a2⁢b2−a3⁢b3)
+ (a0⁢b1+a1⁢b0+a2⁢b3−a3⁢b2)⁢i
+ (a0⁢b2+a2⁢b0+a3⁢b1−a1⁢b3)⁢j
+ (a0⁢b3+a3⁢b0+a1⁢b2−a2⁢b1)⁢k

The multiplication operation on ℍ is defined in such a way that it is compatible with multiplication in the quaternion group Q, and the multiplicative identity is 1=1+0⁢i+0⁢j+0⁢k.

The quaternions are an extension of the complex numbers. If you were to restrict to just the subgroup {1,−1,i,−1}, then the relations satisfied are exactly those satisfied by the complex numbers 1 and i under multiplication. We will soon see that it is this fact which makes quaternions so useful for representing rotations.

Definition 6.2.3.

Let a∈ℍ. Then we define the conjugate of a, denoted a¯, by

a¯=a0−a1⁢i−a2⁢j−a3⁢k,

and the modulus of a, denoted |a| by

|a|2=a⁢a¯=a01+a12+a22+a32
|a|=|a|2.

We can streamline our definition of quaternions by representing a quaternion using a pair of complex numbers rather than a quadruple of real numbers.

Let a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k∈ℍ be a quaternion, then we can equivalently represent it by a pair of complex numbers a=a0+i⁢a1 and b=a2+i⁢a3 using the complex matrix

(a0+i⁢a1a2+i⁢a3−a2+i⁢a3a0−i⁢a1)=(ab−b¯a¯) (6.6)

giving that the quaternions 1,i,j,k have matrices

1↦(1001),i↦(i00−i),j↦(01−10),k↦(0ii0) (6.7)

The product of quaternions is then given by the product of their corresponding matrices.

Definition 6.2.4.

If a quaternion u is such that |u|=1, we call u a unit quaternion. Denote the set of unit quaternions {u∈ℍ||u|=1} by ℍ1.

The unit quaternions ℍ1 form a subgroup of ℍ, since for quaternions a,b in general, the norm of their product is the product of their norms, i.e. |a⁢b|=|a|⁢|b|. This means that for quaternions a,b∈ℍ1, the product |a⁢b−1|=|a||b|=1, which is sufficient to show that ℍ1 is a subgroup of ℍ.

Topologically, the unit quaternions are homeomorphic to the 3-sphere S3, since they are the set of points of unit length in ℝ4. This turns the 3-sphere into a Lie group, isomorphic to SU⁢(2) through identification with their matrices of form 6.6.
