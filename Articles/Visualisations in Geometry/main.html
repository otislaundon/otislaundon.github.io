2.1 Lie Groups and Lie Algebras

In this section we will give some background on the basics of Lie theory, and motivate the importance of studying SO⁡(3) and it’s universal cover SU⁡(2) to the study of Lie groups in general.

2.1.1 Lie Groups

Definition 2.1.

A Lie group is a smooth manifold G together with smooth maps

ρ:G×G →G ι:G →G
(g,h) ↦g⁢h g ↦g−1

and an element e such that for all g,h,k∈G,

(g⁢h)⁢k=g⁢(h⁢k)
g⁢e=g=e⁢g
g⁢g−1=e=g−1⁢g.

A readily available class of Lie groups are the matrix Lie groups.

Definition 2.2.

Let G be a lie group that is a closed subset of GL⁡(n,𝕂). Then we call G a matrix Lie group. [7]

All of the following classical Lie groups are examples of matrix groups [7].

The groups

GL⁡(n,𝕂)=Mat⁡(n×n,𝕂)
SL⁡(n,𝕂)={A∈GL⁡(n,𝕂)|det(A)=1}
O⁡(n,𝕂)={A∈GL⁡(n,𝕂)|A⁢AT=𝕀}
SO⁡(n,𝕂)={A∈GL⁡(n,𝕂)|A⁢AT=𝕀,det(A)=1}
SU⁡(n,ℂ)={A∈GL⁡(n,𝕂)|A⁢A†=𝕀,det(A)=1}

are all matrix lie groups.

An important result mentioned in [7] for understanding the compact Lie groups is

Theorem 2.1.

Let G be a compact Lie group. Then there exists a smooth, injective homomorphism ϕ of G into a general linear group GL⁡(n,ℝ) for some n∈ℕ.

For any element g∈G, we obtain invertible diffeomorphisms Lg,Rg,Cg from G to itself.

Definition 2.3.

Let G be a Lie group, and g∈G. Then we define the left multiplication, right multiplication, and conjugation maps by

Lg:G →G Rg:G →G Cg:G →G
h ↦g⁢h h ↦h⁢g h ↦g⁢h⁢g−1

The left and right multiplication maps as well as the conjugation map are all diffeomorphisms, with inverses Lg−⁢1,Rg−1,Cg−1 respectively. Furthermore, Cg is a group isomorphism for all g∈G.

2.1.2 Lie Algebras

Closely related to the notion of Lie group, is that of a Lie Algebra.

Definition 2.4.

A Lie algebra is a vector space V along with a bilinear, skew-symmetric map [⋅,⋅]:V×V→V such that

[X,[Y,Z]]+[Z,[X,Y]]+[Y,[Z,X]]=0∀X,Y,Z∈V. (1)

Equation (1) is called the Jacobi identity.

Example 2.1.

The set of all vector fields on a smooth manifold forms an infinite-dimensional Lie Algebra under the bracket (X,Y)↦[X,Y] where [X,Y]⁢(f)=X⁢(Y⁢(f))−Y⁢(X⁢(f)).

For any smooth manifold M we have an infinite-dimensional Lie algebra consisting of all vector fields on M. But given a Lie group G, we can obtain a finite dimensional Lie Algebra Lie⁡(G)=𝔤 with dimension equal to that of G. This construction is fundamental to the study of Lie groups.

Keeping with the convention that Lie groups are denoted with upper case letters GL⁡(n,ℝ),SL⁡(n,ℝ),SO⁡(n,ℝ),SU⁡(n), we will denote their corresponding Lie algebras in lower case fraktur 𝔤⁢𝔩,𝔰⁢𝔩,𝔰⁢𝔬,𝔰⁢𝔲, etc.

In order to define Lie⁡(G)=𝔤, we will use left-invariant vector fields.

Definition 2.5.

A left-invariant vector field X∈Γ⁢(T⁢M) is a vector field such that for all g∈G,

(Lg)∗⁢(X)⁢(f)=X⁢(f).

Given a Lie group G, left-invariant vector fields always exist, and are determined uniquely by their value at the identity of G, and because of this, we are able to construct a vector space isomorphism between the set of left-invariant vector fields on a Lie Group to it’s tangent space at the identity.

Proposition 2.1.

Let G be a Lie group. The set of left invariant vector fields on G is a vector space isomorphic to Te⁢G.

Proof.

Let X,Y be left-invariant vector fields and λ be a scalar. Then for all g∈G we have

((ℒg)∗⁢(X+λ⁢Y))⁢(f) =((ℒg)∗⁢X)⁢(f)+(λ⁢(ℒg)∗⁢Y)⁢(f) =X⁢(f)+λ⁢Y⁢(f)

by definition of left-invariance. Therefore the left-invariant vector fields form a vector space. To obtain our isomorphism with Te⁢G, we have map Φ:L→Te⁢G;X↦Xe, and Φ−1:Te⁢G→L;v↦V where Vg=(ℒg)∗⁢v∈TLg⁢(e)⁢G=Tg⁢G. The map Φ is linear because addition and scalar multiplication of vector spaces is defined pointwise. We have that

Φ⁢(Φ−1⁢(v)) =Φ⁢(V)
=Φ⁢(g↦(ℒg)∗⁢v)
=(ℒe)∗⁢v
=id∗⁡v
=v

and

Φ−1⁢(Φ⁢(X)) =Φ−1⁢(Xe)=V

where Vg=(ℒg)∗⁢Xe=Xg by definition of left-invariance. ∎

The above proof is included because it shows us how to obtain a left-invariant vector field from an element of Te⁢G=𝔤, and how to obtain an element of 𝔤 from a left-invariant vector field.

To define the lie bracket on 𝔤, we use the identification of 𝔤 and the set of left invariant vector fields on G, and then use the usual bracket of vector fields.

Definition 2.6.

Let x,y∈𝔤 and X,Y∈Γ⁢(T⁢M) be the unique left invariant vector fields with values Xe=x,Ye=y at the identity. Then

[⋅,⋅]:𝔤×𝔤 →𝔤
(x,y) ↦[X,Y]e

That is we take [x,y] to be the value at the identity of the vector field [X,Y].

Up to covering, every … Lie group is isomorphic to a … Lie group. These correspond one-to-one with … Lie algebras, which are classified by ther Dynkin diagrams. This is only in the complex case…

Every Lie group has a unique Lie algebra Lie⁡(G), but going the other way round is not quite as simple. Each Lie algebra is the Lie algebra of a unique simply connected Lie group, but not a unique Lie group in general.

SU⁡(2) has a dynkin diagram which is just a single vertex with no edges. It is then in some sense the simplest non-abelian Lie group.

2.2 Quaternions

Somewhat backwards to typical fashion, where quaternions find applications in computer graphics [10], we will be applying computer graphics to visualising quaternions.

As a warm up to quaternions, we start with the observation that the Lie group SO⁡(2) of rotations in ℝ2 is isomorphic to {z∈ℂ||z|2=1}, the compact multiplicative subgroup of ℂ\{0}.

Following [14], we introduce quaternions. To do so, we first define the quaternion group

Definition 2.11.

The quaternion group Q is the 8 element set {1,−1,i,−i,j,−j,k,−k} equipped with multiplication such that the relations

i2 =j2=k2=−1
i⁢j =k,

hold. [14]

The quaternion group Q has subgroups

{1}
{1,−1}
Qi= {1,i,−1,−i}=⟨1,i⟩
Qj= {1,j,−1,−j}=⟨1,j⟩
Qk= {1,k,−1,−k}=⟨1,k⟩

where we make note that the subgroups Qi,Qj,Qk each have very natural homomorphisms into the multiplicative group of complex numbers ℂ, each given by their effect on generators

Qi=⟨1,i⟩↦⟨1,i⟩⊂ℂ
Qj=⟨1,j⟩↦⟨1,i⟩⊂ℂ
Qk=⟨1,k⟩↦⟨1,i⟩⊂ℂ

With the quaternion group we are now ready to define the quaternions (or the quaternion algebra).

Definition 2.12.

The quaternions ℍ are the vector space {a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k|a0,a1,a2,a3∈ℝ}. For quaternions a=(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k),b=(b0+b1⁢i+b2⁢j+b3⁢k) and λ∈ℝ we define addition by

(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k)+(b0+b1⁢i+b2⁢j+b3⁢k)=(a0+b0)+(a1+b1)⁢i+(a2+b2)⁢j+(a3+b3)⁢k

and scalar multiplication by

λ⁢(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k)=(λ⁢a0)+(λ⁢a1)⁢i+(λ⁢a2)⁢j+(λ⁢a3)⁢k.

They form an associative algebra with multiplication operation

a⁢b= (a0⁢b0−a1⁢b1−a2⁢b2−a3⁢b3)
+ (a0⁢b1+a1⁢b0+a2⁢b3−a3⁢b2)⁢i
+ (a0⁢b2+a2⁢b0+a3⁢b1−a1⁢b3)⁢j
+ (a0⁢b3+a3⁢b0+a1⁢b2−a2⁢b1)⁢k

[14]

The multiplication operation on ℍ is defined in such a way that it is compatible with multiplication in the quaternion group Q.

The quaternion 1=1+0⁢i+0⁢j+0⁢k is the multiplicitave identity.

As an ℝ-vector space, ℍ≅ℝ4.

The quaternions are an extension of the complex numbers. If you were to restrict to just the subgroup {1,−1,i,−1}, then the relations satisfied are exactly those satisfied by the complex numbers 1 and i under multiplicaiton. We will soon see that it is this fact which makes quaternions so useful for representing rotations.

We can obtain 3 injective algebra homomorphisms from ℂ to ℍ by mapping {1,i} to {1,i}⊂Qi⊂ℍ,{1,j}⊂Qj⊂ℍ or {1,k}⊂Qk⊂ℍ respectively.

We can define a conjugation operation and a norm on ℍ.

Definition 2.13.

Let a∈ℍ. Then we define the conjugate of a, denoted a¯, by

a¯=a0−a1⁢i−a2⁢j−a3⁢k,

and the modulus of a, denoted |a| by

|a|2=a⁢a¯=a01+a12+a22+a32
|a|=|a|2.
Definition 2.14.

A quaternion u is called unitary if |u|=1. Denote the set of unitary quaternions {u∈ℍ||u|=1} by ℍ1. Unitary quaternions are also called versors.

The unitary quaternions ℍ1 form a subgroup of ℍ, since for quaternions a,b in general, the norm of their product is the product of their norms, i.e. |a⁢b|=|a|⁢|b|. This means that for quaternions a,b∈ℍ1, the product |a⁢b−1|=|a||b|=1.

Topologically, the unitary quaternions are homeomorphic to the 3-sphere S3, since they are the set of points of unit length in ℝ4. This turns the 3-sphere into a Lie group, isomorphic to both Spin⁡(3) and SU⁡(2).

We will be using the unitary quaternions to illustrate the double-cover relationship between Spin⁡(3) and SO⁡(3), which corresponds to the fact that the versors u and −u both define the same rotation.

Our map ℍ1→SO⁡(3) is a two-fold covering map, and in fact, as S3 is simply connected, it is the universal cover of SO⁡(3), a fact that will be useful in relation to fundamental groups.

3 Visualisation Techniques

In the interest of defining things rigorously, let Col denote the set of colours.

Let the map RGB:Col→ℝ3 be the map that takes a colour c∈Col to the point RGB⁡(c)=(r,g,b)∈ℝ3 in the standard RGB colorspace with red component r, green component g, and blue component b. Another similar parameterisation for colour is the HSB:Col→S1×ℝ+×ℝ+ which maps a colour to it’s hue, saturaion, and value components in the standard HSB colorspace. Thinking of colours as points in a 3-dimensional space can be useful when thinking about visualisations.

3.1 Domain Colourings

In Farris’s 1998 review [4] of Needham’s book “Visual Complex Analysis” [13], Farris introduces a new way of visualising complex functions of one variable, the technique of domain colouring. The value of this technique for gaining insight about complex functions is mentioned in the paper [5], where we have quote

“it is more closely tied to what students have seen in functions of one and two variables: a diagram that shows the output values assigned to inputs in a domain.”

It is certainly desirable that a visualisation of a function allows you to understand the output for each input in an unambiguous way. And using the graphs of functions can be an extremely useful way of doing this. Here we take a short digression to some set theory.

Definition 3.1.

[2] Let f:X→Y be a function. Then the graph of f is the subset

Γf={(x,f⁢(x))|x∈X}⊆X×Y.

note we slightly deviate from the original notation in [2] to emphasise the fact that the graph of a function is the section of the projection mapping pr1:X×Y→X;(x,y)↦x. This notation Γf is also used in [9]. We take the following definitions from [9] too:

Definition 3.2.

Let X,Y be sets. Then a relation R on X and Y is a subset R⊂X×Y. We say that x∈X and y∈Y are related if (x,y)∈R. In this case, write x⁢R⁢y.

There are are some noteable kinds of relations, such as equivalence relations which are hopefully alerady familiar, and are key in constructing quotients for example. There is another distinguished class of relations called functional relations. Functional relations get their name because they are relations that encode the information of a function.

Definition 3.3.

Let R be a relation on X and Y. If for each x∈X there exists a unique y∈Y such that x⁢R⁢y, then we say that R is a functional relation.

The following theorem from [9] tells us that really, functions and their graphs hold the same information.

Theorem 3.1.

Let X and Y be sets.

  • •

    For every f:X→Y, the graph Γf is a functional relation between X and Y.

  • •

    For every functional relation R between X and Y, there is a unique function f:X→Y such that Γf=R.

In summary, to know the graph of a function, is to know the function itself. This is a principal that will guide much of the visualisation work in this document.

Example 3.1.

In the case that X=Y=ℝ, graphs are what we would typically think of as the graph of a function, for example, the function f:ℝ→ℝ;x↦x2 has graph {(x,f⁢(x))|x∈ℝ}. This elementary example is illustrated in figure 1.

Now, similar to [15], we make rigorous the idea of a domain colouring. Domain colouring is a way of assigning a colour to each point in the domain of a function, for the purpose of visualisation. Functions with easy-to-visualise domains, such as complex functions, are particularly well suited to this approach.

Definition 3.4.

Let X be a set. Then a colour scheme for X is a map κ:Y→Col.

A color scheme could be described in terms of the standard RGB or HSB colour spaces. If X has some extra structure then we may want our colour scheme to somehow preserve this structure. For example, if X is a topological space, then we may want a colour scheme for X to be continuous, or for a colour scheme for a normed vector space we may want to assign brighter colours to vectors with larger norms.

Definition 3.5.

Let f:X→Y be a function between sets X and Y and κ be a colour scheme for Y. Then the domain colouring of f with colour scheme κ is the colour scheme for X given by κ∘f:X→Col.

Now, to make use of this digression into set theory and graphs of functions. In the case where our colour scheme κ for Y is injective, the domain colouring of a function f:X→Y can be thought of as a graph in the sense of set theory, meaning that it tells us everything about the function f. This is in contrast with the usual notion of a graph of a function being a 2 or 3-dimensional plot of a curve or surface sitting above the domain of the function.

Definition 3.6.

Let Y be a set and κ:Y→Col be a colour scheme, Then we call κ faithful if it is injective.

The motivation for definition 3.6 is that it ensures that for functions f,f′:X→Y, their domain colourings κ∘f and κ∘f′ are equal if and only if f=f′. The use of the word faithful then has a similar meaning to in the context of group actions, or more generally functors.

In the case where X=ℂ, it is common to choose a colour scheme for ℂ such that h⁢(κ⁢(z))=arg⁡z, where h:HSB→S1 is the projection that maps a colour in the HSB colour space to it’s hue. [15].

We now see an example of a colour scheme for the complex plane (figure 2), as well as a domain colouring for a complex function using this colour scheme (figure 3).

We can also have domain colourings for functions from the Riemann sphere to itself. This principle is used in 9.

3.2 Double Domain Colourings

In this section we provide an extension to the idea of a color scheme and domain colouring, which particulary in the setting of topology, will allow us visualise a larger class of continuous functions than standard domain colouring.

Sometimes a continuous domain colouring may not be sufficient to properly represent a continuous map, simply because not every topological space admits a continuous faithful domain colouring. If we treat Col as a topological space, it is a contractible 3-dimensional manifold with boundary, homeomorphic to the closed cube I3⊂ℝ3. So any continuous colour scheme for a topological space Y will only be injective if Y can be embedded in I3. For example, if Y is a klein bottle or ℝ⁢P2, then we cannot put a continuous faithful domain colouring on Y.

To get around this, we introduce a double colour scheme, which is a way of assigning a pair of colours to each point in a space rather than just a single colour.

Definition 3.7.

Let Colu2 denote the set of unordered pairs of colours. That is Colu2=Col×Col/∼ where ∼ is the equivalence relation given by (c1,c2)∼(c1′,c2′) if and only if (c1,c2)=(c1′,c2′) or (c1,c2)=(c2′,c1′).

We will use the notation [c,c′] to denote the unordered pair of colours c,c′∈Col in order to distinguish it from the notation (c,c′)∈Col×Col.

In order to visualise an unordered pair of colours [c,c′], we could draw a checkerboard pattern where one set of squares has the colour c and the other has colour c′. See figure 4.

Checkerboards are a good way of representing unordered pairs of colours because swapping which tiles have which colour does not really change how a checkerboard looks. whether we have a black and white checkerboard or a white and black one, it still looks more or less the same.

Using unorderd pairs of colours, we then define a generalisation of a colour scheme.

Definition 3.8.

Let Y be a set. Then a double colour scheme is a map τ:Y→Colu2.

This is a generalisation of the idea of a colour scheme, because if we were to have a double colour scheme τ:Y→Colu2 where for each y∈Y, τ⁢(y)=(c,c) for some c∈C⁢o⁢l, then this would be equivalent to a color scheme on Y.

Now we can see an example of a double colour scheme being put into practice. Consider the following problem. We want to find a continuous colour scheme κ for the 2-sphere S2 which has the property that for all x∈S2, κ⁢(𝐱)=κ⁢(𝐱′) if and only if 𝐱=±𝐱′. This is not possible, because there a colour scheme with this property would be equivalent to an embedding of ℝ⁢P2 into 𝕀3⊂ℝ3, which is not possible. We can however find a faithful (injective) colour scheme for S2. Take for example κ⁢(𝐱)=RGB⁡(x1+12,x2+12,x3+12), which embeds S2 into the RGB colour cube. We can then also consider the colour scheme κ−1 given by κ−1⁢(𝐱):=κ⁢(−𝐱). Using these two colour schemes we can construct the double colour scheme

[κ,κ−1]:S2 →Colu2
𝐱 ↦[κ⁢(𝐱),κ−1⁢(𝐱)].

This now is a double colour scheme with the property that [κ,κ−1]⁢(𝐱)=[κ,κ−1]⁢(𝐱′) if and only if 𝐱=±𝐱′.

We can visualise our double colour scheme by drawing S2, with a checkerboard pattern over it’s surface, and colouring one of the sets of tiles by κ and the other set by κ−1. Figure 5 shows the colour schemes κ and κ−1 as well as the double colour scheme [κ,κ−1]. In the interactive version, drag with the mouse to rotate the spheres to see that the checkerboard colours on antipodal points are indeed the same.

When producing the image of a sphere on screen, there is a composition of maps

S2→ℝ3→I×I

where the first map from S2 to R3 is an embedding of S2 into R3 and the second map, from ℝ3 to I×I, is the projection from ℝ3 on to I×I, which we are using to represent the screen and will be referred to as screen space. Each of these spaces, S2,ℝ3,I×I can be given a checkerboard pattern with which we could visualise a double colour scheme. In figure 5, the checkerboard pattern is rendered in a way that is intrinsic to S2. In figure 6 we see methods of visualising a double colour scheme which use checkerboards coming from ℝ3 and I×I too, compared side-by-side. Axis-aligned great circles are drawn on each sphere to give a visual cue for which side of the sphere is facing the viewer.

This demonstration of different checkerboard patterns being used highlights the fact that the specific pattern used to visualise a double colour scheme τ:Y→Colu2 is not important, only that in some neighbourhood of each point y∈Y both colours from the unordered pair of colours τ⁢(u) are present. Some balance must be stuck between choosing a pattern that is coarse enough that the 2 colours present near any point don’t appear mixed into a single colour to they eye, yet fine enough that we still have each colour present near any given point in Y.

Just as we use colour schemes to define domain colourings, we use double colour schemes to define double domain colourings:

Definition 3.9.

Let f:X→Y be a function and τ:Y→Colu2 be a double colour scheme. Then the double domain colouring of f with double colour scheme τ is the double colour scheme for f given by τ∘f.

An example of a double domain colouring will be seen later on when visualising the two-fold covering map SU⁡(2)→SO⁡(3).

4 Visualising S⁢O⁢(3)

There are two facts that we will later visualise, both of which rely in some way on the Lie group SO⁡(3). Therefore, we will spend some time here describing it, and showing some ways to visualise it.

4.1 Descriptions of SO⁡(3)

First, let’s present some different ways of thinking about SO⁡(3). There are several different ways to describe elements of SO⁡(3), each with their own interpretations, strengths, and weaknesses.

A good place to start is with the definition. Recall that SO⁡(3) is the set of orthogonal 3×3 real matrices with determinant 1. i.e.

SO⁡(3)={A∈Mat⁢(n×n,ℝ)|A⁢AT=𝕀,detA=1}.

Matrices of this form are rotations, and act on column-vectors in ℝ3 by left-multiplication.

Matrices are a convenient way of representing rotations when it comes to computing things. However, they are wasteful. That is, 3×3 matrices use 32=9 numbers to represent a linear transformation, but SO⁡(3) only has dimension 3. If we are only interested in rotations, then much of this information is redundant. There are many ways of describing elements of SO⁡(3) other than by their matrices, such as

  • •

    Angle-axis,

  • •

    Euler-angles,

  • •

    Unit-length quaternions,

  • •

    A subset of the Möbius transformations ℂ→ℂ.

Euler-angles and Angle-axis are each parameterisations of SO⁡(3) which give charts that are dense in SO⁡(3). The methods using Quaternions and Möbius transformations each describe rotations using their own group structure, as well as a homomorphism to SO⁡(3). Below we give more details about each of these descriptions of rotations.

4.1.1 Angle-axis

In this section we build up the theory that formalises the intuitive fact that every rotation is a rotation in ℝ3 about some axis by some angle. In the process, we will also gain some topological insight about SO⁡(3), as well as our first way of visualising SO⁡(3). The steps followed in this section closely follow appendix A of [12].

By the Euler rotation theorem [3], every rotation other than the identity in 3 dimensions fixes some axis. We call this axis the axis of a rotation.

Let θ∈ℝ and 𝐮=(x,y,z)∈ℝ3 be a vector of length 1. Start by defining the skew-symmetric cross matrix of 𝐮

U=(0−zyz0−x−yx0).

We have that

U2=(−(y2+z2)x⁢yx⁢zx⁢y−(x2+z2)y⁢zx⁢zy⁢z−(x2+y2))

and U3=−U,U4=−U2,U5=U and so on such that the odd powers of U are U2⁢n+1=(−1)n⁢U for n≥0 and the even powers are U2⁢n=(−1)n+1⁢U2 for n≥1. Therefore, when we take the matrix exponential

exp⁡(θ⁢U)=∑n=0∞1n!⁢(θ⁢U)n

we can split up the sum into odd and even terms, which gives

𝕀+∑n=0∞1(2⁢n+1)!⁢(θ⁢U)2⁢n+1+∑n=1∞1(2⁢n)!⁢(θ⁢U)2⁢n
= 𝕀+U⁢∑n=0∞(−1)n(2⁢n+1)!⁢θ2⁢n+1+U2⁢∑n=1∞(−1)n+1(2⁢n)!⁢θ2⁢n
= 𝕀+U⁢∑n=0∞(−1)n(2⁢n+1)!⁢θ2⁢n+1+U2⁢(1+∑n=0∞(−1)n+1(2⁢n)!⁢θ2⁢n)
= 𝕀+sin⁡(θ)⁢U+(1−cos⁡θ)⁢U2. (6)

where 𝕀 is the 3×3 identity matrix.

It turns out that this is the rotation matrix which describes the rotation about axis 𝐮 by angle θ.

What we have done is gone from 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3), the set of skew-symmetric real 3×3 matrices, to SO⁡(3). Writing down the cross matrix θ⁢U for vector θ⁢𝐮 is identifying ℝ3 with 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) by mapping θ⁢𝐮=θ⁢(uxuyuz) to the element θ⁢(ux⁢Ux+uy⁢Uy+uz⁢Uz)∈𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) where

Ux=(00000−1010),Uy=(001000−100),Uz=(0−10100000).

Then, using the matrix exponential map, which we know is the same as the Lie algebra exponential map in the case of matrix groups, we get an element of SO⁡(3). This gives us a nice link between angle axis rotations and Lie theory. The angle axis representation (note this is not a representation in the sense of a Lie group representation, just an informal usage of the word representation) of a rotation is it’s logarithmic coordinates, which we know from general theory to be well defined in a neighbourhood of the origin. Indeed, here the logarithmic coordinates are defined on the neighbourhood of 𝕀∈SO⁡(3) that maps to the interior of a ball of radius π in 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3).

We now invoke a lemma from appendix A of [12].

Lemma 4.1.

For any θ∈ℝ and 𝐮 of unit length, we see that exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮) is in SO⁡(3). Conversely, given a rotation R in SO⁡(3), there exists a unique angle θ∈ℝ satisfying 0≤θ≤π and a unit vector 𝐮 such that R=exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮). Furthermore,

  1. 1.

    If 0≤θ<π then 𝐮 is unique,

  2. 2.

    If θ=0 then R=exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮) for any unit vector 𝐮∈ℝ3

  3. 3.

    If ϕ=π, then R=exp⁡(θ⁢𝐯) iff 𝐯=±𝐮.

From lemma 4.1 we can see that every point in SO⁡(3) is exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮) for some θ⁢𝐮∈B0⁢(π)¯, so exp restricted to a closed ball of radius π is surjective.

Furthermore, since the map exp is a continuous map (which is clear from 6), the universal property for quotients gives us a continuous map B0⁢(π)¯/∼→SO(3) where ∼ is the equaliser of exp. The equaliser ∼ of exp is the equivalence relation given by θ⁢𝐮∼θ′⁢𝐱′ if and only if exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮)=exp⁡(θ′⁢𝐮′), which by 4.1 is true iff θ=θ′=π and 𝐮=±𝐮′, and so it is the equivalence relation that identifies antipodal points of the boundary sphere. The quotient space B0⁢(π)¯/∼ is then homeomorphic to ℝ⁢P3.

So we have a continuous map B0⁢(π)¯→SO⁡(3), which is a bijection due to lemma 4.1, and since B0⁢(π)¯/∼ is compact and SO⁡(3)⊂GL⁡(n,ℝ) is Hausdorff, this map is a homeomorphism by Theorem 3.7 from [1]. Therefore, we have that SO⁡(3) is homemorphic to ℝ⁢P3. This tells us that the fundamental group of SO⁡(3) is ℤ/2⁢ℤ, a fact that we will demonstrate in section 7.

4.1.2 Euler Angles

A rotation in R∈ℝ3 can always be described by a composite of 3 axis-aligned rotations. There are many different conventions used for exactly which angles around which axes we compose in which order. To avoid any confusion, the convention we will use here is that any rotation can be described as a rotations R=X⁢(Rx)⁢Y⁢(Ry)⁢Z⁢(Rz), where

X⁢(Rx)=(cos⁡Rx−sin⁡Rx0sin⁡Rxcos⁡Rx0000),Y⁢(Ry)=(cos⁡Ry0−sin⁡Ry000sin⁡Ry0cos⁡Ry),Z⁢(Rz)=(0000cos⁡Rz−sin⁡Rz0sin⁡Rzcos⁡Rz).

The rotation R is then a rotation in the z axis, followed by a rotation in the y axis, followed by a rotation in the x axis.

We will refer to this as the Euler angles representation of a rotation, but the reader should be aware that this is a Tait-Bryan angles version rather than a proper Euler angles [3].

Euler angles provide a maximal chart for SO⁡(3) if we restrict to the set {(Rx,Ry,Rz)∈ℝ3|0≤Rx<π,0≤Ry<π,0≤Rz<2⁢π} then the Euler-angles representation of a rotation is unique.

Möbius transformations are of interest here because they are closely linked with quaternions, and as they are complex functions, can be visualised through domain colourings or other means.

5 Visualising SU⁡(2)

SU⁡(2) is the 2-fold universal cover of SO⁡(3), and is homeomorphic to the 3-sphere S3 [12]. Topologically, S3 can be obtained either by the set {(u,v)∈ℂ2|u2+v2=1}, or by the identification of the boudnary of a closed 3-ball. If we think only topologically first, we know that SU⁡(2) is homeomorphic to S3, and SO⁡(3) is homeomorphic to ℝ⁢P3. 7 Fundamental Group of SO⁡(3)

In this section we will visualise the fact that the fundamental group of SO⁡(3) is ℤ/2⁢ℤ.

We showed in section 6 that SU⁡(2) is the 2-fold cover of SO⁡(3), and gave a concrete description of the covering map Spin:SU⁡(2)→SO⁡(3) as well as a visualisation of the Spin map.

A good way at getting at the fundamental group of a topological space is to look at the group of deck transformations of it’s universal covering space.

Definition 7.1.

[8] For a covering space π:X~→X, the isomorphisms f:X~→X~ (such that π∘f=π) are called deck transformations.

We also have from [8] that the group of deck transformations of a simply-connected locally path-connected covering space X~ is isomorphic to the fundamental group π1⁢(X) (the base point is unimportant due to connectedness of X).

The Spin map has kernel ±1=±(1000)∈ℍ, which means that when we lift a closed path α:I→SO⁡(3) with base point x0 to a path α~:I→SU⁡(2) with base point x0~ with π⁢(x0~)=x0, we get that α~⁢(1) must be ±x0. As there are only 2 possible values for α~⁢(1) here, the group of deck transformations can have at most 2 elements. To see that it has at least 2 elements we only need to show that there exists a loop α:I→SO⁡(3) that lifts to a non-closed path in SU⁡(2), giving a non-trivial element of the group of deck transformations, and therefore a non-trivial element of π1⁢(SO⁡(3)).
