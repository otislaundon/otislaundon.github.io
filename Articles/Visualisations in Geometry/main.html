Abstract

In this project we explore various concepts in the area of differential geometry, topology, and Lie groups through interactive visualisations. Generalising the visualisation technique of domain colouring (typically seen in complex analysis), we use unordered pairs of colours, visualised through checkerboard patterns, which allows for a wider class of continuous functions to be visualised than by standard domain colouring. This technique, combined with standard computer graphics techniques, is then used to create interactive visualisations of the Lie groups SO⁢(3) and SU⁢(2), their left, right, and conjugation self actions, and the double covering of SO⁢(3) by SU⁢(2). We also show visually that the fundamental group of SO⁢(3) is ℤ/2⁢ℤ. Both a PDF and an interactive online version of this document are available. Each version contains the same content, however the online version allows for real-time interaction with the visualisations presented.

Chapter 1 Introduction

 

This document has both a PDF version, and an interactive online version, available at https://otislaundon.com/Articles/Visualisations%20in%20Geometry/main.html. Whilst there are images in the PDF version that show single frames of the interactive visualisations, the reader is highly encouraged to open the interactive version.

 

The Lie groups SO⁢(3) and SU⁢(2), and the relationship between them as groups and their corresponding Lie algebras, are a good showcase for many general concepts encountered in the study of Lie groups. They also find applications in a wide range of fields, from computer graphics [VIN21] to physics [HAM17, NAK03, NAB10]. There are several aspects of these groups for which we create interactive visualisations, which can be accessed in a web browser. These visualisations are embedded into the document as if they are just regular figures, except rather than being still images, they are rendered in real time according to the inputs of the user. We visualise the topologies of SO⁢(3) and SU⁢(2) individually as well as the fact that SU⁢(2) is the universal cover of SO⁢(3), showing how SU⁢(2) maps onto SO⁢(3), how paths in SO⁢(3) lift to paths in SU⁢(2), and that the fundamental group of SO⁢(3) is ℤ/2⁢ℤ. We also visualise the left, right, and conjugation self-actions of SO⁢(3) and SU⁢(2) interactively.

We begin with an introduction to the visualisation technique of domain colouring, typically used for complex functions of one variable, and how it relates to the notion of the graph of a function. We then expand on the technique of domain colouring in a way that uses unordered pairs of colours visualised using checkerboard patterns. This takes us from the 3-dimensional space of colours, to the 6-dimensional space of unordered pairs of colours, which makes it possible to faithfully represent some maps that cannot be using standard domain colouring. Having these techniques at our disposal, as well as standard capabilities of computer graphics for 3D rendering, we then show in detail the geometric, topological, and algebraic properties of SO⁢(3) and SU⁢(2).

A significant amount of work was put into developing a method and workflow for creating a LaTeXdocument which can be compiled to both a PDF and an HTML version, such that the figures present in the PDF version come to life in the HTML version as interactive visualisations. This was achieved through the use of the program LaTeXML [MIL24], with some customisation to allow the images in figures to be replaced with HTML canvases, controlled by JavaScript files.

Throughout this document, we will assume background knowledge knowledge of various areas of mathematics, and use standard definitions from general and algebraic topology from Armstrong [ARM83], Munkres [MUN00], and Hatcher [HAT02], and differential geometry, including manifolds and Lie groups from Hamilton [HAM17] and Hall [HAL15]. Since a significant portion of the content in this document is about Lie groups, some of the basic definitions and constructions used for Lie groups and Lie algebras is included in the appendix 6.

Chapter 2 Visualisation Techniques

2.1 Colour

As we will be producing visualisations to be displayed on a computer monitor, the colours that we have to work with are those which can be produced by a computer monitor. Every monitor will produce colours slightly differently, but the specifics here are not a focus of this project. For our purposes it suffices to think of the gamut (the set of producible colours)[AGO05] of our monitor as the unit cube [0,1]×[0,1]×[0,1]⊂ℝ3 describing the red, green, and blue (RGB) components of a colour. We will denote the set of colours as Col.

Another useful parameterisation of Col is through the HSV (Hue, Saturation, Value) model. As discussed in [AGO05], this way of describing colours arises by looking down the main diagonal of the RGB cube, and parameterising colours in terms of their hue, saturation, and value. The hue of a colour it’s angle around the main diagonal, with the convention that 0 is red, 23⁢π is green, and 43⁢π is blue. For the rest of this document we will assume the hue of a colour to be re-scaled to be in the range [0,1] so that 0,1 are both red, 13 is green, and 23 is blue. The saturation of a colour is its distance from the main diagonal and represents the intensity of the colour, or how much it differs from grey. The value of a colour is how far from black it is. A colour with value 0 is black no matter its hue and saturation, and a colour with value 1 can be white, or any other combination of hue and saturation as long as at least one of the RGB components is 1. Both the saturation and value components are re-scaled to be in the range [0,1].

One important thing to note with HSV components is that not all combinations of h∈[0,1],s∈[0,1],v∈[0,1] produce distinct colours. Instead we have the following:

Remark 2.1.1.

Let C=HSV⁡(hC,sC,vC) and D=HSV⁡(hD,sD,vD) be colours such that C=D. Then one of the following is true:

  • •

    hC=hD and sC=sD and vC=vD,

  • •

    sC=sD=0 and vC=vD,

  • •

    vC=vD=0.

That is to say, if the colour has 0 value, than the other components do not matter, and if the colour has 0 saturation, then the hue component does not matter.

Figure 2.1 shows side-by-side the RGB colour cube, and the HSV colour cylinder. This picture makes remark 2.1.1 clear.

With this perspective on colour, as a subset of ℝ3, we can think of it as a topological space, and consider which maps into Col are continuous for example. This idea is central to the rest of the chapter.

2.2 Domain Colouring

In Farris’s 1998 review [FAR98] of Needham’s book “Visual Complex Analysis” [NEE97], Farris introduces a new way of visualising complex functions of one variable, the technique of domain colouring. Informally, domain colouring works by assigning a colour to each point x in the domain of a function f depending on the output f⁢(x). The value of domain colouring for gaining insight about complex functions is mentioned in the paper [FAR17], where we have quote

“… it is more closely tied to what students have seen in functions of one and two variables: a diagram that shows the output values assigned to inputs in a domain.”

It is certainly desirable that a visualisation of a function allows you to understand the output for each input in an unambiguous way. And using the graphs of functions can be an extremely useful way of doing this. Here we take a short digression to some set theory.

Definition 2.2.1.

[BRI98] Let f:X→Y be a function. Then the graph of f is the subset

Γf={(x,f⁢(x))|x∈X}⊆X×Y.

note we slightly deviate from the original notation in [BRI98] to emphasise the fact that the graph of a function is the section of the projection mapping pr1:X×Y→X;(x,y)↦x. This notation Γf is also used in [LEI24]. We take the following definitions from [LEI24] too:

Definition 2.2.2.

Let X,Y be sets. Then a relation R on X and Y is a subset R⊂X×Y. We say that x∈X and y∈Y are related if (x,y)∈R. In this case, write x⁢R⁢y.

There are are some notable kinds of relations, such as equivalence relations which are used in constructing quotients for example. There is another distinguished class of relations called functional relations. Functional relations get their name because they are relations that encode the information of a function.

Definition 2.2.3.

Let R be a relation on X and Y. If for each x∈X there exists a unique y∈Y such that x⁢R⁢y, then we say that R is a functional relation.

The following theorem from [LEI24] tells us that really, functions and their graphs hold the same information.

Theorem 2.2.1.

Let X and Y be sets.

  • •

    For every f:X→Y, the graph Γf is a functional relation between X and Y.

  • •

    For every functional relation R between X and Y, there is a unique function f:X→Y such that Γf=R.

In summary, to know the graph of a function, is to know the function itself.

Example 2.2.1.

In the case that X=Y=ℝ, graphs are what we would typically think of as the graph of a function, for example, the function f:ℝ→ℝ;x↦x2 has graph {(x,x2)|x∈ℝ}. This elementary example is illustrated in figure 2.2.

Now, similar to [SH15], we make rigorous the idea of a domain colouring. Domain colouring is a way of assigning a colour to each point in the domain of a function, for the purpose of visualisation. Functions with easy-to-visualise domains, such as complex functions of one variable, are particularly well suited to this approach. In order to do domain colouring, we require a way of assigning colours to each point in a set.

Definition 2.2.4.

Let X be a set. Then a colour scheme for X is a map κ:X→Col.

A colour scheme could be described in terms of RGB or HSV components. If X has some extra structure then we may want our colour scheme to somehow preserve this structure. For example, if X is a topological space, then we may want a colour scheme for X to be continuous, or for a colour scheme for a normed vector space we may want to assign brighter colours to vectors with larger norms.

Definition 2.2.5.

Let f:X→Y be a function between sets X and Y and κ be a colour scheme for Y. Then the domain colouring of f with colour scheme κ is the colour scheme for X given by κ∘f:X→Col.

Now, to make use of this digression into set theory and graphs of functions. In the case where our colour scheme κ for Y is injective, the domain colouring of a function f:X→Y can be thought of as the graph of f, where we have

{(x,κ∘f⁢(x))|x∈X}⊆X×κ⁢(Y)⊆X×Col

meaning that it tells us everything about the function f. We can tell exactly where each x∈X maps to in Y by which colour it is assigned because every point in Y is assigned a different colour by κ. We then use the domain colouring of f to visualise f by drawing the domain X (or at least some subset of it) with each point x∈X having colour κ∘f⁢(x). This is in contrast with the usual way of visualising the graph of an ℝ-valued function of 1 or 2 variables, which is done by drawing a 2 or 3-dimensional plot of a curve 2.2 or surface sitting above the domain of the function.

Definition 2.2.6.

Let Y be a set and κ:Y→Col be a colour scheme, Then we call κ faithful if it is injective.

The motivation for definition 2.2.6 is that it ensures that for functions f,f′:X→Y, their domain colouring’s κ∘f and κ∘f′ are equal if and only if f=f′. The use of the word faithful then has a similar meaning to in the context of group actions, or more generally, functors.

We now see an example of a colour scheme κℂ:ℂ→Col for the complex plane given by

κℂ⁢(r⁢ei⁢θ)=HSV⁡(12⁢π⁢θ,min⁡{1,1r},min⁡{1,r}).

This colour scheme is well-defined as r→∞ and has the property that at ∞, in the extended complex plane, that the colour κℂ⁢(∞) is pure white (0 saturation and 1 value). Also, at the origin it is pure black. Being black at the origin, white at ∞, and having the hue be equal to the argument is a common convention for colour schemes for ℂ [SH15]. The colour scheme κℂ is shown for a portion of the complex plane near the origin in (figure 2.3), as well as a domain colouring for a complex function f⁢(z)=z+1+iz−1 using this colour scheme (figure 2.4).

In figure 2.4, we see the pole at z=1 as the white point, and the zero at at −1−i as the black point.

In [PP09] domain colourings are used not only for the complex plane itself, but also for Riemann surfaces and general parameterised surfaces in ℝ3. They also use colour schemes which have some extra features such as radial lines and concentric circles representing level sets of the modulus and argument, which allows information such as conformality to be seen visually. Also by using Riemann surfaces as the domains of their functions, they allow visualisation of multi-valued complex functions such as the square root or logarithm without an arbitrary choice of branch.

2.3 Double Domain Colouring

In this section we provide an extension to the idea of a colour scheme and domain colouring, which particularly in the setting of topology, will allow us visualise a larger class of continuous functions than standard domain colouring.

Sometimes a continuous domain colouring may not be sufficient to properly represent a continuous map, simply because not every topological space admits a continuous faithful domain colouring. If we treat Col as a topological space, it is a contractible 3-dimensional manifold with boundary, homeomorphic to the closed cube I3⊂ℝ3 through the RGB parameterisation. So any continuous colour scheme for a topological space X will only be injective if X can be embedded in I3. For example, if X is a Klein bottle, ℝ⁢P2, or has more than 3 dimensions, then we cannot put a continuous faithful colour scheme on X.

To get around this, we introduce the idea of a double colour scheme, which is a way of assigning a pair of colours to each point in a space rather than just a single colour.

Definition 2.3.1.

Let Colu2 denote the set of unordered pairs of colours. That is Colu2=Col×Col/∼ where ∼ is the equivalence relation given by (c1,c2)∼(c1′,c2′) if and only if (c1,c2)=(c1′,c2′) or (c1,c2)=(c2′,c1′).

We will use the notation [c,c′] to denote the unordered pair of colours c,c′∈Col in order to distinguish it from the notation (c,c′)∈Col×Col for ordered pairs.

In order to visualise an unordered pair of colours [c,c′], we could draw a checkerboard pattern where one set of squares has the colour c and the other has colour c′. See figure 2.5.

Checkerboards are a good way of representing unordered pairs of colours because swapping which tiles have which colour does not really change how a checkerboard looks. whether we have a black and white checkerboard or a white and black one, it still looks more or less the same. Because of this visual indistinguishability between checkerboards with their colours swapped we choose to use unordered pairs of colours for our following generalisation of colour schemes, so that the colour information that can be determined visually is unambiguous near a point.

Definition 2.3.2.

Let X be a set. Then a double colour scheme is a map τ:X→Colu2.

This is in fact a generalisation of the idea of a colour scheme, because if we were to have a double colour scheme τ:X→Colu2 where for each y∈X, τ⁢(x)=[c,c] for some c∈C⁢o⁢l, then this would be equivalent to a colour scheme on X.

Now we can see an example of a double colour scheme being put into practice. Consider the following problem. We want to find a continuous colour scheme κ for the 2-sphere S2 which has the property that for all x∈S2, κ⁢(𝐱)=κ⁢(𝐱′) if and only if 𝐱=±𝐱′. This is not possible, because a continuous colour scheme with this property would be equivalent to an embedding of ℝ⁢P2 (obtained by identifying antipodal points of the 2-sphere) into 𝕀3⊂ℝ3, which is not possible. We can however find a faithful (injective) colour scheme for S2. Take for example κ⁢(𝐱)=RGB⁡(x1+12,x2+12,x3+12), which embeds S2 into the RGB colour cube. We can then also consider the colour scheme κ− given by κ−⁢(𝐱):=κ⁢(−𝐱). Using these two colour schemes we can construct the double colour scheme

[κ,κ−]:S2 →Colu2
𝐱 ↦[κ⁢(𝐱),κ−⁢(𝐱)].

This now is a double colour scheme with the property that [κ,κ−]⁢(𝐱)=[κ,κ−]⁢(𝐱′) if and only if 𝐱=±𝐱′.

We can visualise our double colour scheme by drawing S2, with a checkerboard pattern over it’s surface, and colouring one of the sets of tiles by κ and the other set by κ−. Figure 2.6 shows the colour schemes κ and κ− as well as the double colour scheme [κ,κ−]. In the interactive version, drag with the mouse to rotate the spheres to see that the checkerboard colours on antipodal points are indeed the same.

When producing the image of a 2-sphere on screen using the standard computer graphics pipeline, we can think of the process in a simplified way as a composition of maps

S2→ℝ3→ℝ2

where the first map from S2 to R3 is an embedding of S2 into R3 and the second map, from ℝ3 to ℝ2, is the perspective projection map

(x,y,z)↦(xz,yz).

Then only a rectangular portion of ℝ2, which we identify with I×I is actually drawn to the screen. Each of these spaces, S2,ℝ3,I×I can be given a checkerboard pattern with which we could visualise a double colour scheme. In figure 2.6, the checkerboard pattern is rendered in a way that is intrinsic to S2. In figure 2.7 we see methods of visualising a double colour scheme which use checkerboards coming from ℝ3 and I×I too, compared side-by-side. Axis-aligned great circles are drawn on each sphere to give a visual cue for which side of the sphere is facing the viewer.

This demonstration of different checkerboard patterns highlights the fact that the specific pattern used to visualise a double colour scheme τ:X→Colu2 is not important, only that in some small neighbourhood of each point y∈X both colours from the unordered pair of colours τ⁢(x) are present. Some balance must be stuck between choosing a pattern that is coarse enough that the 2 colours present near any point don’t appear to the eye to be mixed into a single colour, yet fine enough that we still have each colour present near any given point in X.

Just as we use colour schemes to define domain colouring, we use double colour schemes to define double domain colouring:

Definition 2.3.3.

Let f:X→Y be a function and τ:Y→Colu2 be a double colour scheme. Then the double domain colouring of f with double colour scheme τ is the double colour scheme for X given by τ∘f.

Examples of double domain colourings will be seen later in figures 4.2 and4.3 when visualising the 3-sphere being obtained as a quotient of the 3-ball by it’s boundary, and the two-fold covering map SU⁢(2)→SO⁢(3).

Chapter 3 Visualising SO⁢(3)

In future chapters, we will be visualising the relationship between SU⁢(2) and SO⁢(3), and also the fundamental group of SO⁢(3). This chapter serves as both a prerequisite for those future chapters, and as a place to apply the visualisation techniques from the previous chapter, in order to gain a better understanding of the structure of SO⁢(3).

3.1 Descriptions of SO⁢(3)

First, let’s present some different ways of thinking about rotations in 3 dimensions, i.e. elements of SO⁢(3). There are several different ways to describe elements of SO⁢(3), each with their own interpretations, strengths, and weaknesses. A good place to start is with the definition. Recall that SO⁢(3) is the set of orthogonal 3×3 real matrices with determinant 1. i.e.

SO⁢(3)={A∈Mat⁢(3×3,ℝ)|A⁢AT=𝕀,detA=1}.

Matrices of this form represent linear transformations that are rotations, and act on column-vectors in ℝ3 by left-multiplication. Recall also that it’s Lie algebra is

𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3)={A∈Mat⁢(3×3,ℝ)|AT=−A,tr⁢(A)=0},

the set of skew-symmetric traceless 3×3 real matrices.

Matrices are a convenient way of representing rotations when it comes to computing things. However, they are wasteful. That is, 3×3 matrices use 32=9 numbers to represent a linear transformation, but SO⁢(3) only has dimension 3. If we are only interested in rotations, then much of this information is redundant. Some ways of describing elements of SO⁢(3) other than by their matrices are by

  • •

    Angle-axis,

  • •

    Euler-angles,

  • •

    Unit-length quaternions,

  • •

    Möbius transformations ℂ→ℂ (of a certain kind).

The angle-axis and Euler-angles parameterisations of SO⁢(3) give charts that are dense in SO⁢(3). The methods using quaternions and Möbius transformations each describe rotations using their own group structure, as well as a homomorphism to SO⁢(3). Below we give more details about each of these descriptions of rotations.

3.1.1 Angle-axis

Here we introduce the theory that formalises the intuitive fact that every rotation in ℝ3 is a rotation about some axis by some angle. In the process, we will also gain some topological insight about SO⁢(3), as well as a way of visualising SO⁢(3). The steps taken in this section closely follow Appendix A of [NAB10].

By the Euler rotation theorem [CON26], every rotation in 3 dimensions other than the identity fixes a unique axis. We call this the axis of a rotation.

Let θ∈ℝ and 𝐮=(xyz)∈ℝ3 be a vector of length 1. Start by defining the map ϕ:ℝ3→𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) by

ϕ⁢(𝐮)=(0−zyz0−x−yx0). (3.1)

Let U=ϕ⁢(𝐮). Then we have that

U2=(−(y2+z2)x⁢yx⁢zx⁢y−(x2+z2)y⁢zx⁢zy⁢z−(x2+y2))

and U3=−U,U4=−U2,U5=U and so on such that the odd powers of U are U2⁢n+1=(−1)n⁢U for n≥0 and the even powers are U2⁢n=(−1)n+1⁢U2 for n≥1. Therefore, when we take the matrix exponential

exp⁡(θ⁢U)=∑n=0∞1n!⁢(θ⁢U)n

we can split up the sum into odd and even terms, which gives

𝕀+∑n=0∞1(2⁢n+1)!⁢(θ⁢U)2⁢n+1+∑n=1∞1(2⁢n)!⁢(θ⁢U)2⁢n
= 𝕀+U⁢∑n=0∞(−1)n(2⁢n+1)!⁢θ2⁢n+1+U2⁢∑n=1∞(−1)n+1(2⁢n)!⁢θ2⁢n
= 𝕀+U⁢∑n=0∞(−1)n(2⁢n+1)!⁢θ2⁢n+1+U2⁢(1+∑n=0∞(−1)n+1(2⁢n)!⁢θ2⁢n)
= 𝕀+sin⁡(θ)⁢U+(1−cos⁡θ)⁢U2. (3.2)

where 𝕀 is the 3×3 identity matrix.

It turns out that this is the rotation matrix which describes the rotation about axis 𝐮 by angle θ.

What we have done is gone from ℝ3 to 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3), the set of skew-symmetric real 3×3 matrices, and then to SO⁢(3). Writing down the cross matrix θ⁢U for vector θ⁢𝐮 is identifying ℝ3 with 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) by mapping θ⁢𝐮=θ⁢(uxuyuz) to the element θ⁢(ux⁢Ux+uy⁢Uy+uz⁢Uz)∈𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) where

Ux=(00000−1010),Uy=(001000−100),Uz=(0−10100000). (3.3)

Then, using the matrix exponential map, which we know is the same as the Lie algebra exponential map in the case of matrix groups, we get an element of SO⁢(3). This gives us a nice link between the angle axis forms of rotations and Lie theory. The exponential map from 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) to SO⁢(3) has the following properties:

Lemma 3.1.1.

[NAB10] For any θ∈ℝ and 𝐮 of unit length, we see that exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮) is in SO⁢(3). Conversely, given a rotation R in SO⁢(3), there exists a unique angle θ∈ℝ satisfying 0≤θ≤π and a unit vector 𝐮 such that R=exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮). Furthermore,

  1. 1.

    If 0<θ<π then 𝐮 is unique,

  2. 2.

    If θ=0 then R=exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮) for any unit vector 𝐮∈ℝ3

  3. 3.

    If θ=π, then R=exp⁡(θ⁢𝐯) iff 𝐯=±𝐮.

From lemma 3.1.1 we can see that every point in SO⁢(3) is exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮) for some θ⁢𝐮∈B0⁢(π)¯, so exp restricted to a closed ball of radius π is surjective. Going the other way, from SO⁢(3) to B0⁢(π)¯⊂𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3), is the parameterisation by logarithmic coordinates. We know from general theory that logarithmic coordinates for Lie groups are well defined in a neighbourhood of the origin. Indeed, we can see from 3.1.1 they are defined on the neighbourhood of 𝕀∈SO⁢(3) that maps to the interior of a ball of radius π in 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3), which is a maximal dense chart.

Since the map exp is a continuous map (which is clear from 3.2), the universal property of quotients gives us a continuous bijection B0⁢(π)¯/∼→SO(3) where ∼ is the equaliser of exp. The equaliser ∼ of exp is the equivalence relation given by θ⁢𝐮∼θ′⁢𝐱′ if and only if exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮)=exp⁡(θ′⁢𝐮′), which by 3.1.1 is true iff θ=θ′=π and 𝐮=±𝐮′, and so it is the equivalence relation that identifies antipodal points of the boundary sphere. The quotient space B0⁢(π)¯/∼ is homeomorphic to ℝ⁢P3 [HAT02]. So we have a continuous bijection ℝ⁢P3→SO⁢(3), and since B0⁢(π)¯/∼ is compact and SO⁢(3)⊂GL⁢(n,ℝ)⊂ℝn2 is Hausdorff, this map is a homeomorphism by Theorem 3.7 from [ARM83]. Therefore, we have that SO⁢(3) is homeomorphic to ℝ⁢P3. This tells us that the fundamental group of SO⁢(3) is ℤ/2⁢ℤ [NAB10], a fact that we will demonstrate in chapter 4.3.

This angle-axis description of SO⁢(3) will be particularly useful for visualisation, as we can easily draw points in a ball of radius π, the interior of which is a maximal dense chart for SO⁢(3). The main benefit with this technique is that a rotation can be visualised as a single point on screen, rather than needing to show it’s action on some object. This opens up more possibilities for visualising collections of rotations and relationships between them. The main limitation is that the boundary points, corresponding to rotations by π radians about some axis, will not be properly visualised since they will not be represented by a unique point in the visualisation. The viewer must therefore remember that there should be this identification of antipodal points happening on the boundary.

One idea for visualising the identification of antipodal points would be to assign a continuous colour scheme to the 2-sphere in such a way that two points 𝐱,𝐱′ have the same colour if and only if x=±x′. Unfortunately, this is not possible as discussed in 2.3 since such a colour scheme would be equivalent to embedding ℝ⁢P2 into ℝ3. But since there is no injective continuous map ℝ⁢P2→ℝ3, there would necessarily be a pair of non-antipodal points with the same colour.

But recall, we have already described a solution using double colour schemes to this visualisation problem and demonstrated it in Figure 2.6. It is possible to pick an unordered pair of colours at each point on the sphere in such a way that we pick the same pair of colours for antipodal points, and different pairs of colours for each pair of non-antipodal points. And crucially, this can be done in a continuous way.

In Figure 3.1, we pick an element of SO⁢(3) and denote it by θ⁢𝐮 using the angle-axis notation. We then visualise the point θ⁢𝐮 inside the ball of radius π with antipodal points identified (identification shown by double colour scheme [κ,κ−]) on the left hand side of Figure 3.1. Alongside the point θ⁢𝐮 itself, we show the action of θ⁢𝐮 on ℝ3. This is done by showing a cube that is rotated around 𝐮 by angle θ relative to a set of fixed axes. The cube has faces coloured by their normal direction, using the convention that x is red, y is green, and z is blue. For example, the face of the cube that is originally oriented in the positive z direction is blue, and the face in the negative z direction would be yellow, the complimentary colour of blue.

In the interactive version of Figure 3.1, the user can drag with the mouse on the cube on the right hand side of the visualisation to rotate it, thus changing both θ and 𝐮. You can also drag with the mouse on the left hand side of Figure 3.1 to rotate the whole visualisation in order to see it from a different perspective. Pay attention to what happens when θ jumps discontinuously from π to −π (or vice versa). On the left hand side of Figure 3.1 the point θ⁢𝐮 will move suddenly, but on the right hand side we see that this is only an artefact of using a ball with antipodal points identified for the visualisation, as the rotation around 𝐮 by angle θ does not suddenly change as θ goes from π to −π.

We can also extend our double colour scheme from the surface of the ball to the whole ball itself. As it is currently defined, for a point (xyz)∈S2⊂ℝ3, we have colour scheme κ:S2→Col given by RGB⁡(x+12,y+12,z+12). But this formula also defines a faithful colour scheme on B0⁢(1)¯ (which can be scaled up by a factor of π to match our visualisation of SO⁢(3)). We can therefore use κ as before to define a double colour scheme τ:B0⁢(1)¯→Colu2 by

τ⁢(𝐱)=[κ⁢(𝐱),r⁢κ⁢(−𝐱)+(1−r)⁢κ⁢(𝐱)] (3.4)

where r=|x|. Note that when restricted to the boundary of B0⁢(1)¯ it coincides with [κ,κ−]. It is not that obvious that the double colour scheme τ is faithful on the interior, but it can be shown to be due to the asymmetry of how colours κ⁢(𝐱) and κ⁢(−𝐱) are mixed. Rather than using τ as is, which is grey at the centre of B0⁢(1), we attenuate τ to black towards the centre by multiplying the RGB components of each of the colours in τ by r=‖𝐱‖. Define τ0 to be

τ0⁢(𝐱)=[r⁢κ⁢(𝐱),r2⁢κ⁢(−𝐱)+(r−r2)⁢κ⁢(𝐱)]. (3.5)

This then means that darker colours correspond to elements closer to the identity, thus associating the brightness of the colours with the magnitude of the rotations. It also retains the feature that on the surface of the ball, distinct points have the same colour if and only if they are antipodal. When using τ0 to visualise a map into SO⁢(3), it will be clear which points are mapping near to the identity, and which map to rotations by angle π. Also by looking at the hue of the colours, the axis of rotation can be determined. Continuous maps will also have continuous double domain colourings if they use τ0 as their double colour scheme.

In Figure 3.2 we visualise the double colour scheme τ0 for the unit ball. The black great circle on the left hand side of Figure 3.2 shows the cross section (which, with antipodal boundary points identified is homeomorphic to ℝ⁢P2) of B0⁢(1)¯ for which τ0 is visualised on the right hand side of 3.2. By taking cross-sections we get to see the double colour scheme applied to any part of the interior of the ball, which we cannot see from the outside. In the interactive version, drag with the mouse on the left hand side to rotate the view, and drag with the mouse on the right hand side to change which cross section is visualised.

Note that we have not proved that τ0 is faithful. A task for future work is either showing that it is faithful, or constructing a continuous faithful double colour scheme for SO⁢(3) such that it takes value [black,black] at the origin.

3.1.2 Euler Angles

One prevalent way of representing rotations is through Euler angles, which describe a rotation R in ℝ3 by a composite of 3 axis-aligned rotations. There are many different conventions used for exactly which angles around which axes we compose in which order. To avoid any confusion, the convention we will use here is that any rotation can be described as a composite of rotations R=X⁢(Rx)⁢Y⁢(Ry)⁢Z⁢(Rz), where

X⁢(Rx) =(cos⁡Rx−sin⁡Rx0sin⁡Rxcos⁡Rx0000),
Y⁢(Ry) =(cos⁡Ry0sin⁡Ry000−sin⁡Ry0cos⁡Ry),
Z⁢(Rz) =(0000cos⁡Rz−sin⁡Rz0sin⁡Rzcos⁡Rz).

The rotation R is then a rotation in the z axis, followed by a rotation in the y axis, followed by a rotation in the x axis.

We will refer to this as the Euler angles representation of a rotation, but the reader should be aware that this is a Tait-Bryan angles version rather than a proper Euler angles one [CON26].

Euler angles provide a maximal chart for SO⁢(3) if we restrict to the set

{(Rx,Ry,Rz)∈ℝ3|0≤Rx<π,0≤Ry<π,0≤Rz<2⁢π}

then the Euler-angles representation of a rotation is unique. This gives us a way of thinking about rotations as a cube rather than a sphere. We will prefer using the angle-axis representation for our visualisations of SO⁢(3) due to it’s rotational symmetry, and fewer choices (e.g. convention of order of composition) being required.

3.1.3 Möbius Transformations

As in [JS87], let Σ=ℂ∪{∞} denote the extended complex plane, and identify it with the 2-sphere via stereographic projection. Let the unit sphere S2={(x,y,z)∈ℝ3|x2+y2+z2=1}. Then the stereographic projection map π:S2→Σ has formula

π⁢(x,y,z)=x+i⁢y1−z

where in the case z=1, the output should be interpreted as ∞∈Σ.

Definition 3.1.1.

Let f:Σ→Σ. If f is meromorphic and bijective, then we say f is an automorphism of Σ. Denote the group of automorphisms of Σ by Aut⁡(Σ).

Möbius transformations are rational complex functions ℂ→ℂ of the form

z↦a⁢z+bc⁢z+d

where z,a,b,c,d∈ℂ such that a⁢d−b⁢c≠0. In [JS87], the automorphisms of the Riemann sphere are shown to be exactly the Möbius transformations.

There is a surjective group homomorphism GL⁢(2,ℂ)→Aut⁡(Σ) given by

(abcd)↦a⁢z+bc⁢z+d

which has kernel {λ⁢I∈GL⁢(2,ℂ)|λ∈ℂ} [JS87]. It is then a theorem that the Möbius transformations Aut⁡(Σ) form a group isomorphic to the Lie group PGL⁢(2,ℂ) [JS87].

Rotations in ℝ3 (elements of SO⁢(3)) are uniquely determined by their action on S2.

Definition 3.1.2.

[JS87] We define Rot⁡(Σ) to be the set of maps Σ→Σ that are of the form π−1⁢R⁢π where R:S2→S2 is a rotation of the Riemann sphere.

So we have that there is a subgroup Rot⁡(Σ)⊂Aut⁡(Σ) which is isomorphic to SO⁢(3). We then have the theorem

Theorem 3.1.1.

[JS87]

Rot(Σ)={T:z↦a⁢z+b−b¯⁢z+a¯||a|2+|b|2=1}=PSU(2,ℂ).

Using the correspondence between Möbius transformations and 2×2 complex matrices, we get that any matrices of form

(ab−b¯a¯)

define rotations, and moreover two matrices A,B of this form define the same rotation if and only if A=−B which we can see from the fact that

a⁢z+b−b¯⁢z+z¯=−z−bb¯⁢z−z¯.

These matrices are exactly those corresponding to elements of SU⁢(2), or equivalently, unit quaternions.

The way in which Möbius transformations arise from rotations of the Riemann sphere is visualised in Figure 3.3. The complex plane has the domain colouring (with colour scheme κℂ) of the Möbius transformation applied to it which arises from a rotation of the Riemann sphere. The Riemann sphere is also coloured using the domain colouring corresponding to the rotation being applied. The original colour scheme on the Riemann sphere is compatible with that of the plane under stereographic projection. It is given by κℂ∘π:Σ→Col. In the interactive version, drag the mouse to rotate the Riemann sphere and see how the Möbius transformation corresponding to it’s orientation changes. It should be noted that whilst the sphere rotates one way (shown by the orientation of the wire frame around the sphere), the domain colouring appears to rotate the other way. This is no accident, and is a general fact to keep in mind when looking at domain colourings. The motion of the points themselves is inverse to the motion of the colours. This is analogous to the linear equation (x−1) having it’s root at 1, not at −1, which can feel counter intuitive when first learning about linear equations. The root is the point that maps to 0, not the point where 0 maps to.

This can be shown through explicit matrix calculation and some basic results from representation theory.

The standard Lie group representation, which maps A∈SO⁢(3) to the linear map ℝ3→ℝ3 which it defines, has associated Lie algebra representation which takes X∈𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) to the linear map that it defines. The adjoint representation Ad:SO⁢(3)→GL⁢(𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3)) takes the matrix A∈SO⁢(3) to the adjoint map

AdA:𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) →𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3)
X ↦A⁢X⁢A−1

and has associated Lie algebra representation ad:𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3)→GL⁢(𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3)) which maps X∈𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) to adX, which is the map 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3)→𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) given by

adX:𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) →𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3)
Y ↦X⁢Y−Y⁢X.

So it suffices to show that ϕ defined in 3.1 satisfies

ϕ⁢(X⁢𝐯)=adX⁢ϕ⁢(𝐯)

for all

X=(0−cbc0−a−ba0)∈𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3)

and 𝐯=(xyz)∈ℝ3. We have that

ϕ⁢(X⁢𝐯) =ϕ⁢(−c⁢y+b⁢zc⁢x−a⁢z−b⁢x+a⁢y)=(0b⁢x−a⁢yc⁢x−a⁢z−b⁢x+a⁢y0c⁢y−b⁢z−c⁢x+a⁢z−c⁢y+b⁢z0)
=(−c⁢z−v⁢yb⁢xc⁢xa⁢y−c⁢z−a⁢xc⁢ya⁢zb⁢z−b⁢y−a⁢x)−(−c⁢z−b⁢ya⁢ya⁢zb⁢x−c⁢z−a⁢xb⁢zc⁢xc⁢y−b⁢y−a⁢x)
=X⁢ϕ⁢(𝐯)−ϕ⁢(𝐯)⁢X
=adX⁢(ϕ⁢(𝐯)).

Thus, as their associated Lie algebra representations are isomorphic, by proposition 6.1.3, the standard and adjoint representations of SO⁢(3) are isomorphic.

Chapter 4 Visualising SU⁢(2)

Recall that the special unitary group SU⁢(2) is

SU⁢(2)={A∈GL⁢(2,ℂ)|A⁢A†=𝕀,det(A)=1}

where A† is the conjugate transpose of A. More concretely, it is the set of 2×2 complex matrices

(αβ−β¯α¯)

with α,β∈ℂ such that |α|2+|β|2=1 [HAL15], thus making SU⁢(2) homeomorphic to S3. This should be familiar from our earlier discussion of Möbius transformations, where the group Rot⁡(Σ) was shown to be a quotient of this group of matrices. As seen in 6.6, SU⁢(2) is isomorphic to the set of unit quaternions ℍ1.

The Lie algebra 𝔰⁢𝔲⁢(2) is the set of 2×2 skew-Hermitian traceless matrices

𝔰⁢𝔲⁢(2)={A∈GL⁢(2,ℂ)|A†=−A,tr⁢(A)=0},

which is spanned by

u1=(i00−i),u2=(01−10),u3=(0ii0). (4.1)

Topologically, S3 can be obtained by the set {(u,v)∈ℂ2||u|2+|v|2=1}, by the identification of the boundary of a closed 3-ball, or by the one-point compactification of ℝ3. The way which we choose here to visualise it is by a 3-ball of radius π, much like we did for SO⁢(3), since a subset of ℝ3 is easier to conceptualise and visualise than a subset of ℂ2≅ℝ4. We use the exponential map exp:𝔰⁢𝔲⁢(2)→SU⁢(2) to identify the points in B0⁢(π) with elements of SU⁢(2). Note the difference with the ball of radius π being used to visualise SO⁢(3), where we had to identify antipodal points on the boundary, whereas when visualising the 3-sphere in this way we instead need to identify the whole boundary of the 3-ball, collapsing the boundary down to a single point.

When using the basis (4.1) for 𝔰⁢𝔲⁢(2), the exponential map (with image written as a unit quaternion as in 6.7) is

exp⁡(𝐱)=cos⁡(‖𝐱‖)+sin⁡(‖𝐱‖)⁢x⁢i+y⁢j+z⁢k‖𝐱‖

for 𝐱=x⁢u1+y⁢u2+z⁢u3∈𝔰⁢𝔲⁢(2). This function is injective for all 0≤‖𝐱‖<π, and is equal to (−1000) when ‖𝐱‖=π. The exponential map has an inverse (the logarithm) on exp⁡(B0⁢(π)) which gives us a maximal dense chart of SU⁢(2), which is defined on SU⁢(2)\(−1000).

This is analogous to the 2-dimensional case, where we can parameterise the 2-sphere (minus one point) by “wrapping a disc around it”. Figure 4.1 shows how a disk can be wrapped around a sphere, using a domain colouring to show the mapping. The sphere has a faithful colour scheme applied to it, and the disk is mapped onto the sphere such that points with the same colour match up.

In figure 4.1, a continuous faithful colour scheme was put on the 2-sphere. However, as discussed in section 2.3, it is not possible to put a continuous faithful colour scheme on the 3-sphere, since it cannot be embedded into ℝ3. With this in mind, we construct a faithful double colour scheme for S3, so that we can use it to visualise maps into S3.

Our double colour scheme for S3 will have the property that one of the poles (−1000) is just the pair [white,white], and the opposite pole (1000) is the pair [black,black]. This makes the double colour scheme we construct for S3 analogous to the colour scheme for S2 shown in 4.1, and also similar to 3.5, the points closest to the identity in SU⁢(2) will have darkest colours, with the identity itself being black.

Definition 4.0.1.

We define the double colour scheme τS3:S3→Colu2 by its HSV components, given by

τS3⁢(w,x,y,z)=[HSV⁡(h−d⁢h,s,v),HSV⁡(h+d⁢h,s,v)]

where

h =x+14
s =y+12⁢(1−(1−w2)4)
v =1−w2
d⁢h =z+19

and x,y,z,w∈ℝ such that w2+x2+y2+z2=1.

Note that we do indeed have that (−1000) has 0 saturation and 1 value (making it white), and both colours are equal since d⁢h=0, and also that (1000) has 0 value and both colours are equal. Also, each component h,s,v is in the range [0,1]⊂ℝ since w,x,y,z all range from −1 to 1, so they are valid HSV components. Also 0≤d⁢h≤2/9<1/4, meaning that we will not have the issue of distinct values of h giving rise to a pair of colours with hues h−d⁢h and h+d⁢h which are the same.

Proposition 4.0.1.

The double colour scheme τS3 is continuous and faithful.

Proof.

Continuity is clear from construction. Let 𝐚1=(w1,x1,y1,z1) and 𝐚2=(w2,x2,y2,z2). Denote

τS3⁢(𝐚1)=[HSV⁡(h1−d⁢h1,s1,v1),HSV⁡(h1+d⁢h1,s1,v1)]
τS3⁢(𝐚2)=[HSV⁡(h2−d⁢h2,s2,v2),HSV⁡(h2+d⁢h2,s2,v2)].

We can assume d⁢h1,d⁢h2≥0 since our pairs of colours are unordered. Suppose τS3⁢(𝐚1)=τS3⁢(𝐚2). Then v1=v2⟹w1=w2. Since v1=v2=0 if and only if w1=w2=1, and there is only a single point in S3 with w=1, we can assume v1=v2≠0. Then, since v≠0, saturation component is well defined (recall Remark 2.1.1). Therefore s1=s2 which implies y1=y2. Also, the saturation is only 0 when y=−1 (which only happens at a single point) or w=−1 (which also only happens at a single point). Therefore, we can assume that the saturation is non-zero, and so the hue component is well-defined. We then have one of 2 cases:

Case 1: h1−d⁢h1=h2−d⁢h2 and h1+d⁢h1=h2+d⁢h2 giving that

h1=h2+(d⁢h2−d⁢h1)=h2−(d⁢h2−d⁢h1)

which implies that d⁢h2−d⁢h1=0 and so d⁢h2=d⁢h1 and h2=h1.

Case 2: h1−d⁢h1=h2+d⁢h2 and h1+d⁢h1=h2−d⁢h2 giving that

h1=h2−d⁢h2−d⁢h1=h2+d⁢h2+d⁢h1

and so (d⁢h2+d⁢h1)=−(d⁢h2+d⁢h1) giving that d⁢h2=−d⁢h1, but since both d⁢h2,d⁢h1 are non-negative, they must both be 0. Hence d⁢h2=d⁢h1 and h2=h1. In either case we have that h1=h2. The hue component is chosen to only take values in [0,1/2] since the hues 0 and 1 are the same, so we only use half of the range of hues to ensure that distinct x values give rise to distinct hues, and so x1=x2.

Since d⁢h1=d⁢h2, and d⁢h values in the range [0,2/9] give distinct colour pairs (when the value and saturation are nonzero), we get that z1=z2. Therefore, 𝐚1=𝐚2 and so τS3 is injective. ∎

In figure 4.2 we show the double colour scheme τS3 applied to a subset of B0⁢(1)¯ consisting of 3 axis-aligned disks. It is visually apparent that this double colour scheme is continuous, constant on the boundary of B0⁢(1)¯, and otherwise faithful. Therefore, this double colour scheme visually describes the identification of B0⁢(1)¯ which gives rise to the topology of S3 by identifying points with the same unordered pair of colours assigned to them.

The disks seen in figure 4.2 are chosen as a subset of B0⁢(π)¯ on which to visualise the colour scheme τS3, because showing the surface of the ball would be uninteresting as the double colour scheme is constant white over the surface (because exp:𝔰⁢𝔲⁢(2)→SU⁢(2) is constant for vectors of length π).

We remark that the 3-sphere is often visualised through stereographic projection onto ℝ3, which maps the set S3\{(−1000)} bijectively to ℝ3. One benefit of visualising it in this way is that geodesics in the 3-sphere map to circles in ℝ3 under stereographic projection, whereas when using the ball with boundary identified, these circles become distorted. However, one benefit of using the closed ball rather than all of ℝ3 is that both the closed ball and S3 are compact but ℝ3 is not, and so the visualisation using the closed ball conveys this topological property better than if we use stereographic projection. A ball can also be drawn in such a way that every point is in view at once, whereas this is not the case when rendering ℝ3 with 3D perspective, as there will always be points “behind” the viewer.

4.1 Covering map SU⁢(2)→SO⁢(3)

In this section, we provide the definition, and a visualisation the two-fold covering map Spin:SU⁢(2)→SO⁢(3). The material in this section closely follows Appendix A from [NAB10].

If we think only topologically first, we know that SU⁢(2) is homeomorphic to S3, and SO⁢(3) is homeomorphic to ℝ⁢P3. If we take the quotient map Q:S3→ℝ⁢P3 for the equivalence relation that identifies antipodal points in S3, then this is a two-to-one covering map of ℝ⁢P3 by S3. We are interested in a map that is more than just a two-fold covering map though, since SU⁢(2) and SO⁢(3) are Lie groups, we really want our covering map to be a group homomorphism too. This condition is fulfilled by the spinor map.

Definition 4.1.1.

Let U=(w+i⁢xy+i⁢z−y+i⁢zw−i⁢x)∈SU⁢(2). The spinor map Spin:SU⁢(2)→SO⁢(3) is defined by

Spin⁢(U)=(1−2⁢y2−2⁢z22⁢x⁢y+2⁢z⁢w2⁢x⁢z−2⁢y⁢w−2⁢x⁢y+2⁢z⁢w1−2⁢x2−2⁢z22⁢y⁢z+2⁢x⁢w2⁢x⁢z+2⁢y⁢w2⁢y⁢z−2⁢x⁢w1−2⁢x2−2⁢y2)

Definition 4.1.1 is the same as that given in [NAB10], only rewriting diagonal entries using the fact that w2+x2+y2+z2=1.

Theorem 4.1.1.

One way to see this is that SU⁢(2) is isomorphic to Spin⁢(3), the universal covering group of SO⁢(3) [NAK03, TAY54]. Whilst interacting with Figures 3.4, 3.7, 3.8, by rotating the cube on the right hand side in order to change θ⁢𝐮, from one frame to the next a rotation R by a small angle was being composed with the existing rotation b=θ⁢𝐮. We extend this procedure to interactively move around an element b~∈SU⁢(2) as follows:

Start with b=e∈SO⁢(3) and b~=e∈SU⁢(2) so that Spin⁢(b~)=b. Then on each time-step of the interactive visualisation compose b with the rotation R defined by the mouse movement of the user (which will generally be small), and also compose b~ with R~, where R~ is the element of SU⁢(2) corresponding to R under our local isomorphism of SO⁢(3) and SU⁢(2) near the identity. Considering the path in SO⁢(3), with base point at the identity, that b takes as we interact with it, the point b~ will always be the corresponding point on the lifted path in SU⁢(2) which has base point at the identity of SU⁢(2).

Another way of looking at this is that as b moves along the path with velocity controlled by the user’s mouse input, it will always have some velocity in the tangent space to b. We can identify the tangent space to b in SO⁢(3) with the tangent space to b~ through the differential of the Spin map. Then the velocity of b determines the velocity of b~, which accordingly moves along the lifted path.

In this way, we can interact with SU⁢(2) in a very natural way. We can reach any element of SU⁢(2) by a series of compositions of small rotations, each lifted to SU⁢(2). When rotating the cube on the right hand side of 4.4, we modify b and b~ according to the process outlined above.

4.2 Left, Right, and Conjugation Actions on SU⁢(2)

Just as we did for SO⁢(3) in section 3.2, we can demonstrate the group structure of SU⁢(2) by showing the left, right, and conjugation actions of SU⁢(2) on itself, each visualised through it’s effect on a collection of points {ai}1≤i≤k in SU⁢(2).

Figures 4.5, 4.6, 4.7, show the left, right, and conjugation actions of SU⁢(2) on itself by the element b~, which is the lift of the point b as determined by the procedure described in the previous section. The points b and b~ are denoted by blue and red dots respectively. Each of these visualisations has points {ai}1≤i≤k initially randomly selected on spheres of increasing radius. The points {ai} are drawn with a checker pattern which has colours assigned using the double colour scheme τS3.

In the interactive versions of Figure 4.5, the visualisation of Lb~, as we change b~ there is a translational motion of the points in SU⁢(2) in direction 𝐮, and twisting motion anticlockwise around 𝐮. This is very similar to what was observed in 3.4. As we change b=θ⁢𝐮, the twisting motion that takes place in SU⁢(2) in figures 4.5 is only by half of the angle that we change b by, and so the cube on the right hand side must make 2 full rotations before the points on the left hand side return to their original positions. We have at the same time that as b makes one full rotation, the point b~ moves to the outer part of the ball, which corresponds to the hemisphere {(w,x,y,z)∈S3|x<0}. Then after another full rotation of b, b~ and b are together at the identity again.

All of these properties apply to the visualisation in Figure 4.6 of the right action by b~ too, only the twisting action happens in the opposite (clockwise) direction around 𝐮. The translational motion is the same for both the left and right actions because composition of rotations around the same axis is commutative since the set of rotations around a fixed axis form a one-parameter family which is a subgroup of SU⁢(2) isomorphic to the circle group S1, which is commutative.

In the case of the conjugation action in 4.7, we have that Cb~=Lb~∘Rb~−1. This causes the translational motions of Lb~ and Rb~ to cancel out, and the twisting motions which are rotations by half angles in opposite directions to sum to be a rotation by full angle of the change in rotation being applied on the right hand side. The result is that the transformation applied to the points on the left hand side is a rotation, and moreover, it is the same as the rotation of the cube on the right hand side, around axis 𝐮 by angle θ. This shows that the adjoint representation of SU⁢(2) factors through SO⁢(3).

4.3 Fundamental Group of SO⁢(3)

In this section we will visualise the fact that the fundamental group of SO⁢(3) is ℤ/2⁢ℤ.

We know from Section 4.1 that SU⁢(2) is the 2-fold cover of SO⁢(3), and gave a concrete description of the covering map Spin:SU⁢(2)→SO⁢(3) as well as visualised the covering map.

We will be using the fact that paths in SO⁢(3) can be lifted to paths in SU⁢(2). This lifting of paths in SO⁢(3) to paths in SU⁢(2) was the principle used for interacting with SU⁢(2) introduced for the visualisation in Figure 4.4.

A good way at getting at the fundamental group of a topological space is to look at the group of deck transformations of it’s universal covering space.

Definition 4.3.1.

[HAT02] For a covering space π:X~→X, the isomorphisms f:X~→X~ (such that π∘f=π) are called deck transformations.

The group of deck transformations of a simply-connected locally path-connected covering space X~ is isomorphic to the fundamental group π1⁢(X) (the base point is unimportant due to connectedness of X) [HAT02]. In our case, SU⁢(2) is the simply connected, locally path connected covering space of SO⁢(3), and so to find the fundamental group of SO⁢(3) it suffices to find the group of deck transformations.

The Spin map, which is the covering map SU⁢(2)→SO⁢(3) has kernel

±𝕀⊂SU⁢(2)
= ±(1000)⊂ℍ1,

which means that when we lift a closed path α:I→SO⁢(3) with base point x0 to a path α~:I→SU⁢(2) with base point x0~ with π⁢(x0~)=x0, we get that α~⁢(1) must be ±x0. As there are only 2 possible values for α~⁢(1) here, the group of deck transformations can have at most 2 elements. To see that it has at least 2 elements we only need to show that there exists a loop α:I→SO⁢(3) that lifts to a non-closed path in SU⁢(2), giving a non-trivial element of the group of deck transformations, and therefore a non-trivial element of π1⁢(SO⁢(3)). In figure 4.8 we see an example of a path α which is closed in SO⁢(3) which lifts to a non-closed path in SU⁢(2). This shows visually the existence of a non-trivial element of the group of deck transformations. Any group with 2 elements is isomorphic to ℤ/2⁢ℤ, and so π1⁢(SO⁢(3))≅ℤ/2⁢ℤ.

If we concatenate the path α with itself, to get α∗α=α2, then we will have that α2~ is a closed loop. This is because for a nontrivial paths α:I→SO⁢(3) with base point p, the lifted path with base point q∈SO⁢(2) has α~⁢(0)=q and α~⁢(1)=−q. So running through any path twice causes our lifted path to run from q to −q and then back to q, making it guaranteed to be a closed loop. Since SU⁢(2) is simply connected, every closed loop is contractible. In figure 4.9 we see this in practice for the path α from 4.8. As t ranges from 0 to 12, the point α⁢(t) returns to it’s starting point but the point α~⁢(t) now lies in the outer part of the ball (i.e. the hemisphere of S3 with x<0). Then as we make a second pass along α, with t going from 12 to 1, we see that α~⁢(t) returns to the starting point along with α.

This fact can also be visually demonstrated through the Dirac belt trick. To perform this demonstration, take a belt (the kind used to hold up trousers) or some other long strip of material. The belt represents a path in SO⁢(3) where each t∈[0,1], corresponding to a point along the length of the belt, maps to the orientation in space of the surface of the belt near point t. One can introduce a twist of 720 degrees, and then, keeping the orientation of the endpoints of the belt fixed, undo this twist and straighten out the belt, thus showing that a path in SO⁢(3) which makes 2 full rotations is homotopic to the constant path at the identity. This is described and illustrated in [STA10].

A Lie group is a smooth manifold G together with smooth maps

Let G be a Lie group that is a closed subset of GL⁢(n,𝕂). Then we call G a matrix Lie group.

An important result for understanding compact Lie groups is

Theorem 6.1.1.

Let G be a compact Lie group. Then there exists a smooth, injective homomorphism ϕ of G into a general linear group GL⁢(n,ℝ) for some n∈ℕ.

This is saying that every compact Lie group has a faithful representation 6.1.11.

For any element g∈G, we obtain invertible diffeomorphisms Lg,Rg,Cg from G to itself.

h ↦g⁢h h ↦h⁢g h ↦g⁢h⁢g−1

The left and right multiplication maps as well as the conjugation map are all diffeomorphisms, with inverses Lg−1,Rg−1,Cg−1 respectively. Furthermore, Cg is a group isomorphism for all g∈G. These maps for the Lie groups SO⁢(3) and SU⁢(2) are visualised in sections 3.2 and 4.2.

6.1.2 Lie Algebras

Closely related to the notion of Lie group, is that of a Lie Algebra.

Definition 6.1.4.

A Lie algebra is a vector space V along with a bilinear, skew-symmetric map [⋅,⋅]:V×V→V such that

[X,[Y,Z]]+[Z,[X,Y]]+[Y,[Z,X]]=0∀X,Y,Z∈V. (6.1)

Equation (6.1) is called the Jacobi identity.

Example 6.1.1.

The set of all vector fields Γ⁢(T⁢M) on a smooth manifold M forms an infinite-dimensional Lie Algebra under the bracket (X,Y)↦[X,Y] where [X,Y]⁢(f)=X⁢(Y⁢(f))−Y⁢(X⁢(f)).

So for any smooth manifold M we have an infinite-dimensional Lie algebra consisting of all vector fields on M. But given a Lie group G, we can obtain a finite dimensional Lie Algebra Lie⁡(G)=𝔤 with dimension equal to that of G. This construction is fundamental to the study of Lie groups.

Keeping with the convention that Lie groups are denoted with upper case letters GL⁢(n,ℝ), SL⁢(n,ℝ), SO⁢(n,ℝ), SU⁢(n), we will denote their corresponding Lie algebras in lower case fraktur 𝔤⁢𝔩, 𝔰⁢𝔩, 𝔰⁢𝔬, 𝔰⁢𝔲, etc.

In order to define Lie⁡(G)=𝔤, we use left-invariant vector fields.

Definition 6.1.5.

A left-invariant vector field X∈Γ⁢(T⁢M) is a vector field such that for all g∈G,

(Lg)∗⁢(X)⁢(f)=X⁢(f).

We will denote the set of left-invariant vector fields on a Lie group by L.

Given a Lie group G, left-invariant vector fields always exist, and are determined uniquely by their value at the identity of G, and because of this, we are able to construct a vector space isomorphism between the set of left-invariant vector fields on a Lie Group to it’s tangent space at the identity.

Proposition 6.1.1.

Let G be a Lie group. The set L of left invariant vector fields on G is a vector space isomorphic to Te⁢G.

Proof.

Let X,Y be left-invariant vector fields and λ be a scalar. Then for all g∈G we have

((Lg)∗⁢(X+λ⁢Y))⁢(f) =((Lg)∗⁢X)⁢(f)+(λ⁢(Lg)∗⁢Y)⁢(f)
=X⁢(f)+λ⁢Y⁢(f)

by definition of left-invariance. Therefore the left-invariant vector fields form a vector space. To obtain our isomorphism with Te⁢G, we have map

Φ:L →Te⁢G
X ↦Xe

and

Φ−1:Te⁢G →L
v ↦V

where Vg=(Lg)∗⁢v∈TLg⁢(e)⁢G=Tg⁢G. The map Φ is linear because addition and scalar multiplication of vector spaces is defined pointwise. We have that

Φ⁢(Φ−1⁢(v)) =Φ⁢(g↦(Lg)∗⁢v)
=(Le)∗⁢v
=id∗⁡v
=v

and

Φ−1⁢(Φ⁢(X)) =Φ−1⁢(Xe)=V

where Vg=(Lg)∗⁢Xe=Xg by definition of left-invariance. ∎

The above proof is included because it shows us how to obtain a left-invariant vector field from an element of Te⁢G=𝔤, and how to obtain an element of 𝔤 from a left-invariant vector field.

To define the Lie bracket on 𝔤, turning 𝔤 from just a vector space into a Lie algebra, we use the identification of 𝔤 with the set of left invariant vector fields on G, and then use the usual bracket of vector fields.

Definition 6.1.6.

Let x,y∈𝔤 and X,Y∈Γ⁢(T⁢M) be the unique left invariant vector fields with values Xe=x,Ye=y at the identity. Then

[⋅,⋅]:𝔤×𝔤 →𝔤
(x,y) ↦[X,Y]e

That is we take [x,y] to be the value at the identity of the vector field [X,Y].

6.1.3 Exponential Map

Suppose we have a Lie group G with Lie algebra 𝔤. The exponential map is a way of going from 𝔤 to G. It takes a vector 𝐯∈𝔤=Te⁢G and outputs the result of flowing along the left invariant vector field determined by 𝐯 for time 1. Let M be a smooth manifold and X a smooth vector field on M.

Definition 6.1.7.

A curve γ:I→M, where I⊆ℝ is an open interval around 0, is called an integral curve for X through p∈M if

γ⁢(0)=p⁢ and ⁢γ′⁢(t)=Xγ⁢(t)⁢∀t∈I.
Theorem 6.1.2.

For all p∈M, there exists an open neighbourhood U of p in M and an open interval I containing 0 such that the integral curves γq are defined on I for all q∈U. The map

ΦU:U×I →M
(q,t) ↦γq⁢(t)

is differentiable and is called the local flow of X.

Let X∈𝔤 be a left-invariant vector field and let ΦX be the integral curve through through e∈G. Then we define the exponential map

6.1.4 Representations

We can study Lie groups and Lie algebras by how they map into the general linear groups of vector spaces.

Definition 6.1.11.

Let G be a matrix Lie group. A finite-dimensional complex (real) representation of G is a Lie group homomorphism

Π:G→GL⁢(V)

where V is a finite-dimensional complex (real) vector space (with dim(V)≥1). If 𝔤 is real or complex Lie algebra, then a finite-dimensional complex (real) representation of 𝔤 is a Lie algebra homomorphism π:𝔤→𝔤⁢𝔩⁢(V), where V is a finite-dimensional complex (real) vector space.If Π or π is an injective homomorphism, the representation is called faithful.

There is a notion of maps between representations as follows:

Definition 6.1.12.

Let G be a matrix Lie group, let Π be a representation of G acting on the space V, and let Σ be a representation of G acting on the space W. A linear map ϕ:V→W is called an intertwining map of representations if

ϕ⁢(Π⁢(A)⁢v)=Σ⁢(A)⁢ϕ⁢(v)

for all A∈G and all v∈V. The analogous property defines intertwining maps of representations of a Lie algebra.

If ϕ is an intertwining map of representations, and ϕ is invertible, then ϕ is called an isomorphism of representations. If there exists an isomorphism between V and W, then the representations are said to be isomorphic.

Given a representation of a matrix Lie group, we can construct a representation of it’s Lie algebra.

Proposition 6.1.2.

Let G be a matrix Lie group with Lie algebra 𝔤 and let Π be a (finite-dimensional real or complex) representation of G, acting on the space V. Then there is a unique representation π of 𝔤 acting on the same space such that

Π⁢(eX)=eπ⁢(X)

for all X∈𝔤. The representation π can be computed as

π⁢(X)=dd⁢t⁢Π⁢(et⁢X)|t=0

and satisfies

π⁢(A⁢X⁢A−1)=Π⁢(A)⁢π⁢(X)⁢Π⁢(A)−1

for all X∈𝔤 and A∈G.

We then have a way of determining isomorphism of Lie group representations (for connected matrix Lie groups anyway) through their associated Lie algebra representations, which can be easier to work with than the group representations themselves, since the Lie algebras in question are well-behaved finite-dimensional vector spaces.

Proposition 6.1.3.

Let G be a connected matrix Lie group, let Π1 and Π2 be representations of G, and let π1,π2 be the associated Lie algebra representations. Then π1 and π2 are isomorphic if and only if Π1 and Π2 are isomorphic.

Somewhat backwards to typical fashion, where quaternions find applications in computer graphics [VIN21], we are applying computer graphics to visualising quaternions as they appear in representing SU⁢(2).

Following [GIR07], we introduce quaternions. To do so, we first define the quaternion group.

Definition 6.2.1.

The quaternion group Q is the 8 element set {1,−1,i,−i,j,−j,k,−k} equipped with multiplication such that the relations

i2 =j2=k2=−1,
i⁢j =k

hold.

With the quaternion group we are now ready to define the quaternions (or the quaternion algebra).

Definition 6.2.2.

The quaternions ℍ are the vector space {a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k|a0,a1,a2,a3∈ℝ}. For quaternions a=(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k),b=(b0+b1⁢i+b2⁢j+b3⁢k) and λ∈ℝ we define addition by

(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k)+(b0+b1⁢i+b2⁢j+b3⁢k)=(a0+b0)+(a1+b1)⁢i+(a2+b2)⁢j+(a3+b3)⁢k

λ⁢(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k)=(λ⁢a0)+(λ⁢a1)⁢i+(λ⁢a2)⁢j+(λ⁢a3)⁢k.

a⁢b= (a0⁢b0−a1⁢b1−a2⁢b2−a3⁢b3)

+ (a0⁢b2+a2⁢b0+a3⁢b1−a1⁢b3)⁢j
+ (a0⁢b3+a3⁢b0+a1⁢b2−a2⁢b1)⁢k

The multiplication operation on ℍ is defined in such a way that it is compatible with multiplication in the quaternion group Q, and the multiplicative identity is 1=1+0⁢i+0⁢j+0⁢k.

The quaternions are an extension of the complex numbers. If you were to restrict to just the subgroup {1,−1,i,−1}, then the relations satisfied are exactly those satisfied by the complex numbers 1 and i under multiplication. We will soon see that it is this fact which makes quaternions so useful for representing rotations.

Definition 6.2.3.

Let a∈ℍ. Then we define the conjugate of a, denoted a¯, by

and the modulus of a, denoted |a| by

|a|2=a⁢a¯=a01+a12+a22+a32
|a|=|a|2.

We can streamline our definition of quaternions by representing a quaternion using a pair of complex numbers rather than a quadruple of real numbers.

Let a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k∈ℍ be a quaternion, then we can equivalently represent it by a pair of complex numbers a=a0+i⁢a1 and b=a2+i⁢a3 using the complex matrix

(a0+i⁢a1a2+i⁢a3−a2+i⁢a3a0−i⁢a1)=(ab−b¯a¯) (6.6)

giving that the quaternions 1,i,j,k have matrices

1↦(1001),i↦(i00−i),j↦(01−10),k↦(0ii0) (6.7)

The product of quaternions is then given by the product of their corresponding matrices.

Definition 6.2.4.

If a quaternion u is such that |u|=1, we call u a unit quaternion. Denote the set of unit quaternions {u∈ℍ||u|=1} by ℍ1.

The unit quaternions ℍ1 form a subgroup of ℍ, since for quaternions a,b in general, the norm of their product is the product of their norms, i.e. |a⁢b|=|a|⁢|b|. This means that for quaternions a,b∈ℍ1, the product |a⁢b−1|=|a||b|=1, which is sufficient to show that ℍ1 is a subgroup of ℍ.

Topologically, the unit quaternions are homeomorphic to the 3-sphere S3, since they are the set of points of unit length in ℝ4. This turns the 3-sphere into a Lie group, isomorphic to SU⁢(2) through identification with their matrices of form 6.6.
