Abstract

In this project we explore various concepts in the area of differential geometry, topology, and Lie groups through interactive visualisations. Generalising the visualisation technique of domain colourings (typically seen in complex analysis), we introduce a technique that uses unordered pairs of colours, visualised through checkerboard patterns, allowing a wider class of continuous functions to be visualised than by standard domain colourings. This technique, combined with more traditional plotting, is then used to visualise the Lie groups SO⁢(3) and SU⁢(2), as well as their left, right, and conjugation maps, and the double covering of SO⁢(3) by SU⁢(2). We also show visually that the fundamental group of SO⁢(3) is ℤ/2⁢ℤ.

Chapter 2 Visualisation Techniques

Let Col denote the set of visible colours. Let the map RGB:Col→ℝ3 be the map that takes a colour c∈Col to the point RGB⁡(c)=(r,g,b)∈ℝ3 in the standard RGB colorspace with red component r, green component g, and blue component b. Another similar parameterisation for colour is the HSB:Col→S1×ℝ+×ℝ+ which maps a colour to it’s hue, saturaion, and value components in the standard HSB colorspace. Thinking of colours as points in a 3-dimensional space can be useful when thinking about visualisations.

2.1 Domain Colourings

In Farris’s 1998 review [4] of Needham’s book “Visual Complex Analysis” [14], Farris introduces a new way of visualising complex functions of one variable, the technique of domain colouring. The value of this technique for gaining insight about complex functions is mentioned in the paper [5], where we have quote

“it is more closely tied to what students have seen in functions of one and two variables: a diagram that shows the output values assigned to inputs in a domain.”

It is certainly desirable that a visualisation of a function allows you to understand the output for each input in an unambiguous way. And using the graphs of functions can be an extremely useful way of doing this. Here we take a short digression to some set theory.

Definition 2.1.1.

[2] Let f:X→Y be a function. Then the graph of f is the subset

Γf={(x,f⁢(x))|x∈X}⊆X×Y.

note we slightly deviate from the original notation in [2] to emphasise the fact that the graph of a function is the section of the projection mapping pr1:X×Y→X;(x,y)↦x. This notation Γf is also used in [10]. We take the following definitions from [10] too:

Definition 2.1.2.

Let X,Y be sets. Then a relation R on X and Y is a subset R⊂X×Y. We say that x∈X and y∈Y are related if (x,y)∈R. In this case, write x⁢R⁢y.

There are are some noteable kinds of relations, such as equivalence relations which are hopefully alerady familiar, and are key in constructing quotients for example. There is another distinguished class of relations called functional relations. Functional relations get their name because they are relations that encode the information of a function.

Definition 2.1.3.

Let R be a relation on X and Y. If for each x∈X there exists a unique y∈Y such that x⁢R⁢y, then we say that R is a functional relation.

The following theorem from [10] tells us that really, functions and their graphs hold the same information.

Theorem 2.1.1.

Let X and Y be sets.

  • •

    For every f:X→Y, the graph Γf is a functional relation between X and Y.

  • •

    For every functional relation R between X and Y, there is a unique function f:X→Y such that Γf=R.

In summary, to know the graph of a function, is to know the function itself.

Example 2.1.1.

In the case that X=Y=ℝ, graphs are what we would typically think of as the graph of a function, for example, the function f:ℝ→ℝ;x↦x2 has graph {(x,x2)|x∈ℝ}. This elementary example is illustrated in figure 2.1.

Now, similar to [16], we make rigorous the idea of a domain colouring. Domain colouring is a way of assigning a colour to each point in the domain of a function, for the purpose of visualisation. Functions with easy-to-visualise domains, such as complex functions, are particularly well suited to this approach.

Definition 2.1.4.

Let X be a set. Then a colour scheme for X is a map κ:X→Col.

A color scheme could be described in terms of the standard RGB or HSB colour spaces, which parameterise the set of visible colours by their red, green, and blue components in the case of RGB, or hue, saturation, and brightness components. If X has some extra structure then we may want our colour scheme to somehow preserve this structure. For example, if X is a topological space, then we may want a colour scheme for X to be continuous, or for a colour scheme for a normed vector space we may want to assign brighter colours to vectors with larger norms.

Definition 2.1.5.

Let f:X→Y be a function between sets X and Y and κ be a colour scheme for Y. Then the domain colouring of f with colour scheme κ is the colour scheme for X given by κ∘f:X→Col.

Now, to make use of this digression into set theory and graphs of functions. In the case where our colour scheme κ for Y is injective, the domain colouring of a function f:X→Y can be thought of as a graph in the sense of set theory, meaning that it tells us everything about the function f. We can tell exactly where each x∈X maps to in Y by which colour it is assigned because every point in Y is assigned a different colour. This is in contrast with the usual notion of a graph of a function being a 2 or 3-dimensional plot of a curve or surface sitting above the domain of the function.

Definition 2.1.6.

Let Y be a set and κ:Y→Col be a colour scheme, Then we call κ faithful if it is injective.

The motivation for definition 2.1.6 is that it ensures that for functions f,f′:X→Y, their domain colourings κ∘f and κ∘f′ are equal if and only if f=f′. The use of the word faithful then has a similar meaning to in the context of group actions, or more generally, functors.

Domain colourings were originally introduced to visualse complex functions of one variable, and so in the context of complex analysis it is common to choose a colour scheme for the extended complex plane ℂ∪{∞} such that the hue of the colour assigned to z∈ℂ corresponds to the argument of z and the brightness of the colour corresponds to the modulus |z| such that the brightness of 0∈ℂ is 0, and the brightness of ∞ in the extended complex plane is 1 (i.e. pure white). [16].

We now see an example of a colour scheme for the complex plane (figure 2.2), as well as a domain colouring for a complex function using this colour scheme (figure 2.3).

In [9] domain colourings are used not only for the complex plane itself, but also for Riemann surfaces and general parameterised surfaces in ℝ3. They also use colour schemes which have some extra features such as radial lines and concentric circles representing level sets of the modulus and argument, which allows information such as conformality to be seen visually. Also by using Riemann surfaces as the domains of their functions, they allow visualisation of multi-valued complex functions such as the square root or logarithm without an arbitrary choice of branch.

2.2 Double Domain Colourings

In this section we provide an extension to the idea of a color scheme and domain colouring, which particulary in the setting of topology, will allow us visualise a larger class of continuous functions than standard domain colourings.

Sometimes a continuous domain colouring may not be sufficient to properly represent a continuous map, simply because not every topological space admits a continuous faithful domain colouring. If we treat Col as a topological space, it is a contractible 3-dimensional manifold with boundary, homeomorphic to the closed cube I3⊂ℝ3 through the RGB parameterisation. So any continuous colour scheme for a topological space Y will only be injective if Y can be embedded in I3. For example, if Y is a klein bottle, ℝ⁢P2, or has more than 3 dimensions, then we cannot put a continuous faithful colour scheme on Y.

To get around this, we introduce a double colour scheme, which is a way of assigning a pair of colours to each point in a space rather than just a single colour.

Definition 2.2.1.

Let Colu2 denote the set of unordered pairs of colours. That is Colu2=Col×Col/∼ where ∼ is the equivalence relation given by (c1,c2)∼(c1′,c2′) if and only if (c1,c2)=(c1′,c2′) or (c1,c2)=(c2′,c1′).

We will use the notation [c,c′] to denote the unordered pair of colours c,c′∈Col in order to distinguish it from the notation (c,c′)∈Col×Col for ordered pairs.

In order to visualise an unordered pair of colours [c,c′], we could draw a checkerboard pattern where one set of squares has the colour c and the other has colour c′. See figure 2.4.

Checkerboards are a good way of representing unordered pairs of colours because swapping which tiles have which colour does not really change how a checkerboard looks. whether we have a black and white checkerboard or a white and black one, it still looks more or less the same. Because of this visual indistinguishability between checkerboards with their colours swapped we choose to use unordered pairs of colours for our following generalisation of colour schemes.

Definition 2.2.2.

Let Y be a set. Then a double colour scheme is a map τ:Y→Colu2.

This is in fact a generalisation of the idea of a colour scheme, because if we were to have a double colour scheme τ:Y→Colu2 where for each y∈Y, τ⁢(y)=[c,c] for some c∈C⁢o⁢l, then this would be equivalent to a color scheme on Y.

Now we can see an example of a double colour scheme being put into practice. Consider the following problem. We want to find a continuous colour scheme κ for the 2-sphere S2 which has the property that for all x∈S2, κ⁢(𝐱)=κ⁢(𝐱′) if and only if 𝐱=±𝐱′. This is not possible, because there a colour scheme with this property would be equivalent to an embedding of ℝ⁢P2 into 𝕀3⊂ℝ3, which is not possible. We can however find a faithful (injective) colour scheme for S2. Take for example κ⁢(𝐱)=RGB⁡(x1+12,x2+12,x3+12), which embeds S2 into the RGB colour cube. We can then also consider the colour scheme κ−1 given by κ−1⁢(𝐱):=κ⁢(−𝐱). Using these two colour schemes we can construct the double colour scheme

[κ,κ−1]:S2 →Colu2
𝐱 ↦[κ⁢(𝐱),κ−1⁢(𝐱)].

This now is a double colour scheme with the property that [κ,κ−1]⁢(𝐱)=[κ,κ−1]⁢(𝐱′) if and only if 𝐱=±𝐱′.

We can visualise our double colour scheme by drawing S2, with a checkerboard pattern over it’s surface, and colouring one of the sets of tiles by κ and the other set by κ−1. Figure 2.5 shows the colour schemes κ and κ−1 as well as the double colour scheme [κ,κ−1]. In the interactive version, drag with the mouse to rotate the spheres to see that the checkerboard colours on antipodal points are indeed the same.

When producing the image of a 2-sphere on screen, there is a composition of maps

S2→ℝ3→I×I

where the first map from S2 to R3 is an embedding of S2 into R3 and the second map, from ℝ3 to I×I, is the projection from ℝ3 on to I×I, which we are using to represent the screen and will be referred to as screen space. Each of these spaces, S2,ℝ3,I×I can be given a checkerboard pattern with which we could visualise a double colour scheme. In figure 2.5, the checkerboard pattern is rendered in a way that is intrinsic to S2. In figure 2.6 we see methods of visualising a double colour scheme which use checkerboards coming from ℝ3 and I×I too, compared side-by-side. Axis-aligned great circles are drawn on each sphere to give a visual cue for which side of the sphere is facing the viewer.

This demonstration of different checkerboard patterns being used highlights the fact that the specific pattern used to visualise a double colour scheme τ:Y→Colu2 is not important, only that in some neighbourhood of each point y∈Y both colours from the unordered pair of colours τ⁢(u) are present. Some balance must be stuck between choosing a pattern that is coarse enough that the 2 colours present near any point don’t appear mixed into a single colour to they eye, yet fine enough that we still have each colour present near any given point in Y.

Just as we use colour schemes to define domain colourings, we use double colour schemes to define double domain colourings:

Definition 2.2.3.

Let f:X→Y be a function and τ:Y→Colu2 be a double colour scheme. Then the double domain colouring of f with double colour scheme τ is the double colour scheme for f given by τ∘f.

An example of a double domain colouring will be seen later on when visualising the two-fold covering map SU⁢(2)→SO⁢(3).

As mentioned already, we can define a double colour scheme for a set X by a pair of maps κ1,κ2:X→Col and then taking for each point x∈X the unordered pair [κ1⁢(x),κ2⁢(x)]. This works well in some situations such as 2.5. The main downside to defining double colour schemes in this way is that it is more difficult in general to show that they are faithful, since if equality of unordered pairs splits into the cases

[a,b]=[a′,b′]⟹{a=a′⁢ and ⁢b=b′⁢ or⁢a=b′⁢ and ⁢b=a′.

To avoid having to check multiple cases we can parameterise a portion of Colu2 by the average and difference of each component.

Definition 2.2.4.

Let RGBdRGB be the parameterisation of Colu2 given by

RGBdRGB⁡(r,g,b,d⁢r,d⁢g,d⁢b)=[RGB⁡(r+d⁢rg+d⁢gb+d⁢b),RGB⁡(r−d⁢rg−d⁢gb−d⁢b)] (2.1)

where r,g,b∈[0,1]⊂ℝ and d⁢r∈[0,min⁡{r,1−r}]⊂ℝ, d⁢g∈[0,min⁡{g,1−b}]⊂ℝ, d⁢b∈[0,min⁡{b,1−b}⊂ℝ].

We use min⁡{r,1−r}, and similar for g,b, so that we always have r−d⁢r,r+d⁢f∈[0,1] since we requre that the red, green, and blue components are all in [0,1]. Note the symbols d⁢r,d⁢g,d⁢b are just denoted as such because they are “differences” from r,g,b, not because they are any kind of differential.

Proposition 2.2.1.

The RGBdRGB parameterisation of Colu2 is injective for r,g,b∈[0,1] and d⁢r∈[0,min⁡{r,1−r}], d⁢g∈[0,min⁡{g,1−b}], d⁢b∈[0,min⁡{b,1−b}].

Proof.

First let’s show injectivity. Suppose RGBdRGB⁡(r,g,b,d⁢r,d⁢g,d⁢b)=RGBdRGB⁡(r′,g′,b′,d⁢r′,d⁢g′,d⁢b′). Then we have 2 possible cases.

We only show that r=r′ and d⁢r=d⁢r′, but the same method can be applied for the g and b components.

Case 1: r+d⁢r=r′+d⁢r′ and r−d⁢r=r′−d⁢r′, giving that

r=r′+(d⁢r′−d⁢r)=r′−(d⁢r′−d⁢r)

which implies that d⁢r′−d⁢r=0 and so d⁢r′=d⁢r and r=r′.

Case 2: r+d⁢r=r′−d⁢r′ and r−d⁢r=r′+d⁢r′, giving that

r=r′−d⁢r′−d⁢r=r′+d⁢r′+d⁢r

and so (d⁢r′+d⁢r)=−(d⁢r′+d⁢r) giving that d⁢r′=−d⁢r, but since both d⁢r,d⁢r′ are non-negative, they must both be 0. Hence d⁢r=d⁢r′ and r=r′. ∎

Note that we only have injectivity and not surjectivity here because unordered pairs [RGB⁡(r1,g1,b1),RGB⁡(r2,g2,b2)]∈Colu2 with r1>r2 and g1<g2 (as well as other permutations of the signs of r1−r1, g1−g2, b1−b2) cannot be reached by RGBdRGB.

Similar to the RGBdRGB we can define HSVdHSV, which again takes in 6 parameters for the averages and differences of the hue, saturation, and value colour components.

Definition 2.2.5.

We define the HSVdHSV parameterisation of a portion of Colu2 by

HSVdHSV⁡(h,s,v,d⁢h,d⁢s,d⁢v)=[HSV⁡(h+d⁢h,s+d⁢s,v+d⁢v),HSV⁡(h−d⁢h,s−d⁢s,v−d⁢v)] (2.2)

Where h∈S1={e2⁢π⁢i⁢t|t∈[0,1)} which we identify with the set, s∈[0,1]⊂R, v∈[0,1]⊂ℝ and d⁢h∈[0,π/2]⊂ℝ, d⁢s∈[0,min⁡{s,1−s}]⊂ℝ, d⁢v∈[0,min⁡{v,1−v}].

We have that HSVdHSV is injective where it is defined.

Chapter 3 Visualising SO⁢(3)

There are two facts that we will later visualise, both of which rely in some way on the Lie group SO⁢(3). Therefore, we will spend some time here describing it, and showing some ways to visualise it.

3.1 Descriptions of SO⁢(3)

First, let’s present some different ways of thinking about SO⁢(3). There are several different ways to describe elements of SO⁢(3), each with their own interpretations, strengths, and weaknesses.

A good place to start is with the definition. Recall that SO⁢(3) is the set of orthogonal 3×3 real matrices with determinant 1. i.e.

SO⁢(3)={A∈Mat⁢(n×n,ℝ)|A⁢AT=𝕀,detA=1}.

Matrices of this form are rotations, and act on column-vectors in ℝ3 by left-multiplication.

Matrices are a convenient way of representing rotations when it comes to computing things. However, they are wasteful. That is, 3×3 matrices use 32=9 numbers to represent a linear transformation, but SO⁢(3) only has dimension 3. If we are only interested in rotations, then much of this information is redundant. There are many ways of describing elements of SO⁢(3) other than by their matrices, such as

  • •

    Angle-axis,

  • •

    Unit-length quaternions,

  • •

    A subset of the Möbius transformations ℂ→ℂ.

The angle-axis parameterisation of SO⁢(3) gives a chart that is dense in SO⁢(3). The methods using Quaternions and Möbius transformations each describe rotations using their own group structure, as well as a homomorphism to SO⁢(3). Below we give more details about each of these descriptions of rotations.

3.1.1 Angle-axis

In this section we build up the theory that formalises the intuitive fact that every rotation is a rotation in ℝ3 about some axis by some angle. In the process, we will also gain some topological insight about SO⁢(3), as well as a way of visualising SO⁢(3). The steps followed in this section closely follow appendix A of [13].

By the Euler rotation theorem [3], every rotation in 3 dimensions other than the identity fixes a unique axis. We call this the axis of a rotation.

Let θ∈ℝ and 𝐮=(x,y,z)∈ℝ3 be a vector of length 1. Start by defining the skew-symmetric cross matrix of 𝐮

U=(0−zyz0−x−yx0).

We have that

U2=(−(y2+z2)x⁢yx⁢zx⁢y−(x2+z2)y⁢zx⁢zy⁢z−(x2+y2))

and U3=−U,U4=−U2,U5=U and so on such that the odd powers of U are U2⁢n+1=(−1)n⁢U for n≥0 and the even powers are U2⁢n=(−1)n+1⁢U2 for n≥1. Therefore, when we take the matrix exponential

exp⁡(θ⁢U)=∑n=0∞1n!⁢(θ⁢U)n

we can split up the sum into odd and even terms, which gives

𝕀+∑n=0∞1(2⁢n+1)!⁢(θ⁢U)2⁢n+1+∑n=1∞1(2⁢n)!⁢(θ⁢U)2⁢n
= 𝕀+U⁢∑n=0∞(−1)n(2⁢n+1)!⁢θ2⁢n+1+U2⁢∑n=1∞(−1)n+1(2⁢n)!⁢θ2⁢n
= 𝕀+U⁢∑n=0∞(−1)n(2⁢n+1)!⁢θ2⁢n+1+U2⁢(1+∑n=0∞(−1)n+1(2⁢n)!⁢θ2⁢n)
= 𝕀+sin⁡(θ)⁢U+(1−cos⁡θ)⁢U2. (3.1)

where 𝕀 is the 3×3 identity matrix.

It turns out that this is the rotation matrix which describes the rotation about axis 𝐮 by angle θ.

What we have done is gone from 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3), the set of skew-symmetric real 3×3 matrices, to SO⁢(3). Writing down the cross matrix θ⁢U for vector θ⁢𝐮 is identifying ℝ3 with 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) by mapping θ⁢𝐮=θ⁢(uxuyuz) to the element θ⁢(ux⁢Ux+uy⁢Uy+uz⁢Uz)∈𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) where

Ux=(00000−1010),Uy=(001000−100),Uz=(0−10100000).

Then, using the matrix exponential map, which we know is the same as the Lie algebra exponential map in the case of matrix groups, we get an element of SO⁢(3). This gives us a nice link between angle axis rotations and Lie theory. The angle axis representation (note this is not a representation in the sense of a Lie group representation, just an informal usage of the word representation) of a rotation is it’s logarithmic coordinates, which we know from general theory to be well defined in a neighbourhood of the origin. Indeed, here the logarithmic coordinates are defined on the neighbourhood of 𝕀∈SO⁢(3) that maps to the interior of a ball of radius π in 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3).

The exponential map from 𝔰⁢𝔬⁢(3) to SO⁢(3) has the following properties:

Lemma 3.1.1.

[13] For any θ∈ℝ and 𝐮 of unit length, we see that exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮) is in SO⁢(3). Conversely, given a rotation R in SO⁢(3), there exists a unique angle θ∈ℝ satisfying 0≤θ≤π and a unit vector 𝐮 such that R=exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮). Furthermore,

  1. 1.

    If 0<θ<π then 𝐮 is unique,

  2. 2.

    If θ=0 then R=exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮) for any unit vector 𝐮∈ℝ3

  3. 3.

    If θ=π, then R=exp⁡(θ⁢𝐯) iff 𝐯=±𝐮.

From lemma 3.1.1 we can see that every point in SO⁢(3) is exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮) for some θ⁢𝐮∈B0⁢(π)¯, so exp restricted to a closed ball of radius π is surjective.

Furthermore, since the map exp is a continuous map (which is clear from 3.1), the universal property for quotients gives us a continuous map B0⁢(π)¯/∼→SO(3) where ∼ is the equaliser of exp. The equaliser ∼ of exp is the equivalence relation given by θ⁢𝐮∼θ′⁢𝐱′ if and only if exp⁡(θ⁢𝐮)=exp⁡(θ′⁢𝐮′), which by 3.1.1 is true iff θ=θ′=π and 𝐮=±𝐮′, and so it is the equivalence relation that identifies antipodal points of the boundary sphere. The quotient space B0⁢(π)¯/∼ is then homeomorphic to ℝ⁢P3.

So we have a continuous map B0⁢(π)¯→SO⁢(3), which is a bijection due to lemma 3.1.1, and since B0⁢(π)¯/∼ is compact and SO⁢(3)⊂GL⁢(n,ℝ) is Hausdorff, this map is a homeomorphism by Theorem 3.7 from [1]. Therefore, we have that SO⁢(3) is homemorphic to ℝ⁢P3. This tells us that the fundamental group of SO⁢(3) is ℤ/2⁢ℤ, a fact that we will demonstrate in section 6.

This angle-axis description of SO⁢(3) will be particularly useful for visualisation, as we can easily draw points in a ball of radius π, the interior of which is a maximal dense chart for SO⁢(3). The main limitation is that the boundary points, corresponding to rotations by π radians about some axis, will not be properly visualised. The viewer must remember that there should be this identification of antipodal points on the boundary happening.

One way that we could visualise the identification of antipodal points would be to assign a continuous colour scheme to the 2-sphere in such a way that two points 𝐱,𝐱′ have the same colour if and only if x=±x′. Unfortunately, however, this is not possible as discussed in 2.2 as such a colour scheme would be equivalent to embedding ℝ⁢P2 into ℝ3. But since there is no injective continuous map ℝ⁢P2→ℝ3, there would necessarily be a pair of non-antipodal points with the same colour.

We have already described the solution to this visualisaiton problem and demonstrated it in figure 2.5. It is possible to pick a pair of colours at each point on the sphere in such a way that we pick the same pair of colours for antipodal points, and different pairs of colours for each pair of non-antipodal points.

In figure 3.1, we pick an element of SO⁢(3) and denote it by θ⁢𝐮 using the angle-axis notation. We then visualise the point θ⁢𝐮 inside the ball of radius π with antipodal points identified (identification shown by double colour scheme). Also, alongside the point θ⁢𝐮 itself, we show the action of θ⁢𝐮 on ℝ3. This is done by showing a cube that is rotated around 𝐮 by angle θ relative to a set of fixed axes. The cube has faces coloured by their normal direction, using the convention that x is red, y is green, and z is blue. For example, the face of the cube that is originally oriented in the positive z direction is blue, and the face in the negative z direction would be yellow, the complimentary colour of blue.

Proof.

Though τ4⁢(x,y,z,w) is an unordered pair of colours, as long as they are not both 0, we can identify which came from x,y and which from z,w by checking if the blue component is non-zero. 8.1 Lie Groups and Lie Algebras

In this section we will give some background on the basics of Lie theory, and motivate the importance of studying SO⁢(3) and it’s universal cover SU⁢(2) to the study of Lie groups in general.

8.2 Lie Groups

Definition 8.2.1.

A Lie group is a smooth manifold G together with smooth maps

ρ:G×G →G ι:G →G
(g,h) ↦g⁢h g ↦g−1

and an element e such that for all g,h,k∈G,

(g⁢h)⁢k=g⁢(h⁢k)
g⁢e=g=e⁢g
g⁢g−1=e=g−1⁢g.

A readily available class of Lie groups are the matrix Lie groups.

Definition 8.2.2.

Let G be a lie group that is a closed subset of GL⁢(n,𝕂). Then we call G a matrix Lie group. [7]

All of the following classical Lie groups are examples of matrix groups [7].

Example 8.2.1.

Let 𝕂 denote a field ℝ or ℂ. The groups

GL⁢(n,𝕂)=Mat⁡(n×n,𝕂)
SL⁢(n,𝕂)={A∈GL⁢(n,𝕂)|det(A)=1}
O⁢(n,𝕂)={A∈GL⁢(n,𝕂)|A⁢AT=𝕀}
SO⁢(n,𝕂)={A∈GL⁢(n,𝕂)|A⁢AT=𝕀,det(A)=1}
SU⁢(n,ℂ)={A∈GL⁢(n,𝕂)|A⁢A†=𝕀,det(A)=1}

are all matrix lie groups.

An important result for understanding compact Lie groups is

Theorem 8.2.1.

[7] Let G be a compact Lie group. Then there exists a smooth, injective homomorphism ϕ of G into a general linear group GL⁢(n,ℝ) for some n∈ℕ.

This is saying that every compact Lie group has a faithful representation.

For any element g∈G, we obtain invertible diffeomorphisms Lg,Rg,Cg from G to itself.

Definition 8.2.3.

Let G be a Lie group, and g∈G. Then we define the left multiplication, right multiplication, and conjugation maps by

Lg:G →G Rg:G →G Cg:G →G
h ↦g⁢h h ↦h⁢g h ↦g⁢h⁢g−1

The left and right multiplication maps as well as the conjugation map are all diffeomorphisms, with inverses Lg−⁢1,Rg−1,Cg−1 respectively. Furthermore, Cg is a group isomorphism for all g∈G. These maps will all be visualised for the Lie groups SO⁢(3) and SU⁢(2) later in this document.

8.2.1 Lie Algebras

Closely related to the notion of Lie group, is that of a Lie Algebra.

Definition 8.2.4.

A Lie algebra is a vector space V along with a bilinear, skew-symmetric map [⋅,⋅]:V×V→V such that

[X,[Y,Z]]+[Z,[X,Y]]+[Y,[Z,X]]=0∀X,Y,Z∈V. (8.1)

Equation (8.1) is called the Jacobi identity.

Example 8.2.2.

The set of all vector fields Γ⁢(T⁢M) on a smooth manifold M forms an infinite-dimensional Lie Algebra under the bracket (X,Y)↦[X,Y] where [X,Y]⁢(f)=X⁢(Y⁢(f))−Y⁢(X⁢(f)).

So for any smooth manifold M we have an infinite-dimensional Lie algebra consisting of all vector fields on M. But given a Lie group G, we can obtain a finite dimensional Lie Algebra Lie⁡(G)=𝔤 with dimension equal to that of G. This construction is fundamental to the study of Lie groups.

Keeping with the convention that Lie groups are denoted with upper case letters GL⁢(n,ℝ), SL⁢(n,ℝ), SO⁢(n,ℝ), SU⁢(n), we will denote their corresponding Lie algebras in lower case fraktur 𝔤⁢𝔩, 𝔰⁢𝔩, 𝔰⁢𝔬, 𝔰⁢𝔲, etc.

In order to define Lie⁡(G)=𝔤, we use left-invariant vector fields.

Definition 8.2.5.

A left-invariant vector field X∈Γ⁢(T⁢M) is a vector field such that for all g∈G,

(Lg)∗⁢(X)⁢(f)=X⁢(f).

We will denote the set of left invariant vector fields on a Lie group by L.

Given a Lie group G, left-invariant vector fields always exist, and are determined uniquely by their value at the identity of G, and because of this, we are able to construct a vector space isomorphism between the set of left-invariant vector fields on a Lie Group to it’s tangent space at the identity.

Proposition 8.2.1.

Let G be a Lie group. The set L of left invariant vector fields on G is a vector space isomorphic to Te⁢G.

Proof.

Let X,Y be left-invariant vector fields and λ be a scalar. Then for all g∈G we have

((Lg)∗⁢(X+λ⁢Y))⁢(f) =((Lg)∗⁢X)⁢(f)+(λ⁢(Lg)∗⁢Y)⁢(f)
=X⁢(f)+λ⁢Y⁢(f)

by definition of left-invariance. Therefore the left-invariant vector fields form a vector space. To obtain our isomorphism with Te⁢G, we have map

Φ:L →Te⁢G
X ↦Xe

and

Φ−1:Te⁢G →L
v ↦V

where Vg=(Lg)∗⁢v∈TLg⁢(e)⁢G=Tg⁢G. The map Φ is linear because addition and scalar multiplication of vector spaces is defined pointwise. We have that

Φ⁢(Φ−1⁢(v)) =Φ⁢(g↦(ℒg)∗⁢v)
=(ℒe)∗⁢v
=id∗⁡v
=v

and

Φ−1⁢(Φ⁢(X)) =Φ−1⁢(Xe)=V

where Vg=(ℒg)∗⁢Xe=Xg by definition of left-invariance. ∎

The above proof is included because it shows us how to obtain a left-invariant vector field from an element of Te⁢G=𝔤, and how to obtain an element of 𝔤 from a left-invariant vector field.

To define the Lie bracket on 𝔤, turning 𝔤 from just a vector space to a Lie alhebra, we use the identification of 𝔤 with the set of left invariant vector fields on G, and then use the usual bracket of vector fields.

Definition 8.2.6.

Let x,y∈𝔤 and X,Y∈Γ⁢(T⁢M) be the unique left invariant vector fields with values Xe=x,Ye=y at the identity. Then

[⋅,⋅]:𝔤×𝔤 →𝔤
(x,y) ↦[X,Y]e

That is we take [x,y] to be the value at the identity of the vector field [X,Y].

Every Lie group has a unique Lie algebra Lie⁡(G), but going the other way round is not quite as simple. Each Lie algebra is the Lie algebra of a unique simply connected Lie group, but not a unique Lie group in general.

Up to covering, every … Lie group is isomorphic to a … Lie group. These correspond one-to-one with … Lie algebras, which are classified by ther Dynkin diagrams. This is only in the complex case…

SU⁢(2) has a dynkin diagram which is just a single vertex with no edges. It is then in some sense the simplest non-abelian Lie group.

8.3 Quaternions

Somewhat backwards to typical fashion, where quaternions find applications in computer graphics [11], we will be applying computer graphics to visualising quaternions as they appear in representing SU⁢(2).

As a warm up to quaternions, we start with the observation that the Lie group SO⁢(2) of rotations in ℝ2 is isomorphic to {z∈ℂ||z|2=1}, a compact multiplicative subgroup of ℂ\{0}.

Following [15], we introduce quaternions. To do so, we first define the quaternion group.

Definition 8.3.1.

[15] The quaternion group Q is the 8 element set {1,−1,i,−i,j,−j,k,−k} equipped with multiplication such that the relations

i2 =j2=k2=−1
i⁢j =k,

hold.

With the quaternion group we are now ready to define the quaternions (or the quaternion algebra).

Definition 8.3.2.

[15] The quaternions ℍ are the vector space {a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k|a0,a1,a2,a3∈ℝ}. For quaternions a=(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k),b=(b0+b1⁢i+b2⁢j+b3⁢k) and λ∈ℝ we define addition by

(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k)+(b0+b1⁢i+b2⁢j+b3⁢k)=(a0+b0)+(a1+b1)⁢i+(a2+b2)⁢j+(a3+b3)⁢k

and scalar multiplication by

λ⁢(a0+a1⁢i+a2⁢j+a3⁢k)=(λ⁢a0)+(λ⁢a1)⁢i+(λ⁢a2)⁢j+(λ⁢a3)⁢k.

They form an associative algebra with multiplication operation

a⁢b= (a0⁢b0−a1⁢b1−a2⁢b2−a3⁢b3)
+ (a0⁢b1+a1⁢b0+a2⁢b3−a3⁢b2)⁢i
+ (a0⁢b2+a2⁢b0+a3⁢b1−a1⁢b3)⁢j
+ (a0⁢b3+a3⁢b0+a1⁢b2−a2⁢b1)⁢k

The multiplication operation on ℍ is defined in such a way that it is compatible with multiplication in the quaternion group Q, and the multiplicitive identity is 1=1+0⁢i+0⁢j+0⁢k.

The quaternions are an extension of the complex numbers. If you were to restrict to just the subgroup {1,−1,i,−1}, then the relations satisfied are exactly those satisfied by the complex numbers 1 and i under multiplicaiton. We will soon see that it is this fact which makes quaternions so useful for representing rotations.

Definition 8.3.3.

Let a∈ℍ. Then we define the conjugate of a, denoted a¯, by

a¯=a0−a1⁢i−a2⁢j−a3⁢k,

and the modulus of a, denoted |a| by

|a|2=a⁢a¯=a01+a12+a22+a32
|a|=|a|2.
Definition 8.3.4.

If a quaternion u is such that |u|=1, we call u a unit quaternion. Denote the set of unit quaternions {u∈ℍ||u|=1} by ℍ1. Unit quaternions are also called versors.

The unit quaternions ℍ1 form a subgroup of ℍ, since for quaternions a,b in general, the norm of their product is the product of their norms, i.e. |a⁢b|=|a|⁢|b|. This means that for quaternions a,b∈ℍ1, the product |a⁢b−1|=|a||b|=1, which is sufficient to show that ℍ1 is a subgroup of ℍ.

Topologically, the unit quaternions are homeomorphic to the 3-sphere S3, since they are the set of points of unit length in ℝ4. This turns the 3-sphere into a Lie group, isomorphic to SU⁢(2).
